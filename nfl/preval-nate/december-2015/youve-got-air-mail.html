You've Got Air Mail

The NFL's leading passers through week twelve

by Nate Preval

Share Tweet

Now that we are almost thirteen weeks deep into this NFL season, we can start crunching the numbers. In this particular article, I want to understand which quarterbacks have really stood out this season. Normally, this type of discussion immediately begins revolving around statistical measures like passing yards and completion percentage, but in order to fully recognize a QB’s performance, we will need to dig even deeper to see which starting quarterback is the biggest deep threat up to this point.

New England Patriots quarterback and Ugg boots model Tom Brady leads the league in passing yards through week twelve, with San Diego’s Phillip Rivers and Arizona’s Carson Palmer trailing him by at least one hundred yards a piece. But stats-driven folks know that there is more to passing yards than just the ball being thrown in air. There are yards after completion (YAC), which depict a receiver’s contributions to their quarterback’s stat line in terms of yards gained after a completion is made. At this point, any quarterback who thrives in an offense that likes to put the pass underneath the secondary, a more “pounding away” type of play, cannot expect to make explosive plays week in and week out just solely with their arm. Wide receivers will always do their job to go above and beyond and make that spectacular play but once a week we see them catch the ball for a short gain and then suddenly break out into a highlight reel like Odell Beckham Jr. did against the Patriots this season.

So when interpreting QB passing metrics data, eliminating the YAC stat would result in lesser passing yardage numbers since the “passing yards” stats, without YAC, only account for yards gained as a byproduct of the quarterback’s pass from the line of scrimmage to completion. If the receiver goes on a slaloming 50-yard run after the pass is completed, so be it, but it won’t be counted in our analysis of the best deep threat QBs. Many of the same top quarterbacks that were seen in the prior set are still there like Brady, Rivers, Drew Brees, and Russell Wilson. Meanwhile, some of those among the league leaders in passing yards kindly dropped off: Eli and Peyton Manning, Cam Newton, and even the excellent Aaron Rodgers.

Well, that’s a surprise. Our Uggs model is garnering a lot of his passing yards thanks to having three yards-after-catch workhorses on his team: Rob Gronkowski (423 YAC, 4th in the league), Dion Lewis (338 YAC, 14th in the league), and Julian Edelman (327 YAC, 15th in the league). Compare this to rookie quarterback Jameis Winston, who has a lower overall passing yards number, but somehow excels despite his receivers lacking abilities in garnering those all-important yards-after-completions. Winston is regularly shepherding a Bucs offense that has YAC letdowns Charles Sims (252 YAC, 31st in the league) and Mike Evans (177 YAC, 80th in the league).

Now you’re probably wondering…man, even someone like the currently injured Bryan Hoyer likes taking his chances downfield? Exactly. We always see highlights of the big play, and criticize those that do not take their chances. Actually, I cannot recall a single game watched where the supposed sportscaster or analyst did not criticize the team for taking their chances down the field or exposing the opportunity of doing so. The quarterbacks in the NFL now rely on two very important things when it comes to decision making and play calling: their coaching staff and on-field gamesmanship. Only a quarterback who is trusted by their staff can decide to run a play that can be risky going deep down the field. However, coaches do not catch the full feel of a game like the players do. For example: If Jameis Winston has been noticing the subtle tendencies of the secondary to leave the middle of the field open, he probably will suggest a play down the field, deep, to his favorite target this year, Mike Evans.

Now when we understand this data, there is one other data set to look into before making a choice: yards per attempt (YPA). On average, the best QB’s tend to have a number awfully close to 10, the normal amount of yards to the next first down. Currently, averaging a yards per attempt rating of 9.04 is - cue the drum roll - Carson Palmer. Tom Brady, the one player that perennially has the efficiency of one of the greatest to ever play, is currently ranked fourth, behind Ben Roethlisberger and Andy Dalton. Despite a lackluster season in Seattle, Russell Wilson checks in at 5th in the league in yards per attempt. Other notables that float around in this dataset of YPA include Matt Ryan, Eli Manning and Drew Brees.

This should leave you puzzled: Why haven’t the Cardinals gotten more big-time publicity despite being first in their division?

Carson Palmer is easily having one of the best seasons of his career right now, but he’s going completely unnoticed in comparison to the likes of Tom Brady or Cam Newton, and even Russell Wilson. At 27 TD’s and 9 interceptions, he is a 35-year-old QB with a mission to take his team to the top. Right now, Arizona is poised to be the only competition left in the NFC for a steam-rolling Carolina Panthers, who also happen to be the NFL’s worst undefeated team.

Unfortunately, the Cards have lost some force behind their red machine of domination; they lost Chris Johnson and Andre Ellington to the injured reserve list. With their fleet of veteran leadership and rookie raw talent, the Arizona Cardinals are third in the power rankings led by their veteran QB in Palmer, who should be tied for first among Newton and Brady for this season’s MVP award, because the stats certainly back his case. One cannot fully make an argument for the whole team, with their losses having exposed their lack of energy on both sides of the ball, however, there is no disputing what Carson Palmer has been able to do both under the glaring Glendale Sun and on the road this season.

To quote a certain Oklahoma City Thunder superstar and former MVP: Carson, you the real MVP.

Nate Preval
