The Mamba’s Last Strike

As Kobe Bryant inches towards retirement, his impact on the NBA will pervade the game’s very core

by Viraj Patel

Share Tweet

Well, I never thought this moment would arrive so soon. Sure, the warning signs had been appearing these past few years as the injuries piled up. His repeated outbursts towards his teammates — “You mofos are soft like Charmin”—were clearly simmering with the frustration of an aging superstar. Or they were just red herrings. Maybe the injuries were just setbacks that were leading to a great eventual comeback? Maybe the outbursts were the Mamba’s own outlandish measures to keep his teammates in line? Maybe the living legend was not going to become a dying legend riding off into the sunset of retirement?

I never thought the moment would arrive when I’d have to refer to Kobe’s career as something that occurred in the past. He’s been my favorite NBA player ever since I began watching the sport and it’s not just because of those hilarious, nonsensical #KobeSystem ads. (You’re welcome.)

His most alluring quality to me was his work ethic. We’ve all heard the stories of Kobe’s relentless pursuit of those championships and how much he’s sacrificed to get to those momentary moments of elation and joy. We’ve also heard about Kobe’s drive to log the basketball equivalent of “10,000 hours” at all hours of the day. Screw 10,000 hours. He’s probably posted close to a 100,000 by now. But when his NBA career wraps up, what will Kobe Bryant be remembered for?

Will it be the five championships? Will it be his 17 all-star selections? Will it be his 11 all-NBA first team selections? Or will we remember the nine all-NBA Defensive first team selections? Or his 1997 NBA Slam Dunk Contest championship? Or his two NBA regular season scoring titles? Or the 81-point game? Or, above all else, will we only evaluate his career compared to that of Michael Jordan’s?

Chances are we’ll do all of the above. But ultimately, it won’t matter. What will matter is the effect he had on shaping the hearts and minds of this generation’s NBA stars. Last night after Kobe announced his retirement, we immediately began hearing the platitudes coming in from the current crop of NBA superstars. Paul George of the Indiana Pacers said, “Kobe was my Jordan.” Kevin Durant of the Oklahoma City Thunder chimed in by saying, “I did idolize Kobe Bryant. I studied him, wanted to be like him. He was our Michael Jordan.”

Even the living legends of other sports supplied their fond remembrances of the Mamba’s illustrious career.

In the sports media world, the likes of Stephen A. Smith led the praises while the likes of Colin Cowherd led the derision brigade. ESPN’s J.A. Adande asserted that “Kobe was the bridge from the Michael Jordan era until the likes of LeBron James, Kevin Durant and Stephen Curry took control.” The Los Angeles Times’s Bill Plaschke, on the other hand, took a more dour, but realistic look on the Lakers superstar’s harsh reality:

The theme of the poem is that he is leaving the game he loves. The reality is that he is also leaving a game that no longer loves him. Though the 37-year-old Bryant has hinted since summer that he would choose to make this his final season, it recently became obvious that he had no choice.

According to the media, Kobe has every right to be placed in the upper echelons of basketball’s pantheon of greats, but he has no right to continue beyond this 20th and final season. They’ve seen enough of the Black Mamba. But where does that leave us, the fans?

It leaves us exactly where we’d hoped to be when Kobe neared the end of his career. Even though he kept repeatedly stating how unsure of retirement he was this season, we never really knew whether to cheer for him in these final games or boo him for his tepid 1-for-14 nights. We were never sure whether to covet his “final” season or to unabashedly knock him for possibly holding the Lakers hostage for another “big money” contract once this year’s deal ended. We were caught in this maelstrom of wanting to root for Kobe while simultaneously being frustrated by his shoddy shooting nights.

But now the frustrations of the past give way to the knowledge of the present. And with that knowledge, the path forward becomes clearer. Barring any long-term injuries, we now know we only have 66 more games to watch a 37-year-old Kobe lead a 2–14 Lakers team. Knowing that makes all the difference. It provides a sense of foreboding while also providing a sense of comfort in knowing there’s an imminent conclusion to Kobe’s story. It doesn’t mean the journey will be enjoyable, it just means we get 66 final chances to watch greatness navigate the unrelenting arcs of basketball, aging, and the resulting emotional and physical scars that this process ostensibly leaves.

I’m ready for the next 66, Mamba.

I think.
