The T-Wolves Look to Break from the Pack

Although team efficiency stats depict a thoroughly average team, the Timberwolves’ young stars supply the individual firepower

by Viraj Patel

Share Tweet

Through their first eighteen games of the 2015–2016 NBA season, the Minnesota Timberwolves are the embodiment of average. They sit just below the .500 mark with an underwhelming 8–10 record in a tough Western Conference led by a perfect 20–0 Golden State Warriors team playing a level of basketball rarely exhibited on the hardwood. While the parity is better, surprisingly, in the East — where only four wins separate the best team (Cavaliers, 13–5 record) and the 11th best team (Knicks, 9–10) — the West still boasts more powerhouse sides like the aforementioned Warriors, Spurs (16–4), and the Thunder (11–8).

So far, the Wolves have only played the Dubs, the Clippers, and the Grizzlies from the West’s best, but they’ve lost all three games by an average of 9.6 points. This trend makes sense since the Warriors boast a league-leading offensive rating of 113.7, and the Clippers are not far behind in fifth place with an offensive rating of 103.8. Both teams also play suffocating defense that nicely supplements their offensive prowesses. The 11-9 Grizzlies, on the other hand, appear in the bottom half of the league leaders in terms of both their offensive (99.9) and defensive (102.6) ratings, so the Wolves should have done better in their 114–106 loss at the Target Center to Memphis.

What about the Wolves themselves, though? This team checks in near the bottom half of almost every team efficiency stat the NBA provides. For example, the Timberwolves have an offensive rating of 100.6 (17th best) and a defensive rating of that same amount (15th best). They’re 17th in turnover ratio and they’re in the bottom third of the league in effective field goal percentage (47.6%). To provide context of the eFG%, the Memphis Grizzlies are the bottom dwellers of this stat with their disappointing 45.7% eFG%.

Minnesota is 15th in pace — the number of possessions per 48 minutes — and they’re 20th in assists ratio — the number of assists a team averages per 100 of their own possessions. All in all, the cumulonimbus clouds of average are swooping in already during this long, cold winter in Minneapolis.

Surely, there are some things the Wolves are doing as a team that are above average, right? They do have a 8–10 record and sit in the eighth spot in a usually tough Western Conference, so it makes sense that while their performances have been lackluster, there are some positives in place. From a team perspective, the Wolves are the eighth best team in the NBA in terms of defensive rebounds percentage (thank you, Karl Anthony-Towns), which also makes them the eighth best in overall rebounds percentage. Although the team ranks in the bottom half in terms of assists percentage and assist-to-turnover ratio, in twelve games, Ricky Rubio has posted 8.0 assists per game, which is doing wonders for the Wolves’ young stars Andrew Wiggins and Anthony-Towns in terms of getting a few uncontested shots every game.

Speaking of Wiggins, the 20-year-old Canadian has quietly posted 22.1 PPG (10th best in the NBA) this season. And his 18.14 PER rating is bested only by Anthony-Towns (20.56), Andre Miller, Ph.D. (18.51), and Zach LaVine (18.22). His own strengths — slashing to the rim for those rim-rattling dunks and shooting midrange shots from the pick-and-roll — mirror those of his team: The Wolves are the 7th best team in the league with 6.1 field goals made per game from 15–19 feet out. Likewise, Wiggins’s dreadful 3-pt shooting percentage of 30.4 perfectly captures his team’s similarly nightmarish affinity to jack up 8.1 3-pt attempts per game while converting on only 1.9 of them. The Wolves’s 23.3% 3-pt success metric would be dead last in the league had South Beach’s Miami Heat not done marginally worse with their own 20.9% stinker.

Sometimes, as evident in the highlight above, the Wolves deviate from the mean and start punishing teams with LaVine’s spacing and Wiggins’s midrange abilities. When they’re fully flowing and hitting their stride, they can contest with virtually any team in the NBA, but these scoring outbursts are few and far in between. And, as if on cue, the Wolves immediately regressed back to the mean over the next few days as they suffered losses against the Clippers and the Magic.

Following the tragic death of their head coach, Flip Saunders, earlier this season, the Timberwolves have struggled to perform under the tutelage of Sam Mitchell. But the signs of improvement are slowly starting to appear. For instance, the Wolves are in the top ten, or just shy of that, in their defensive efficiency when teams take shots on them from 10 to 24 feet. This is a testament to the tireless efforts of Wiggins, LaVine, and Karl Anthony-Towns to aggressively close down opposing teams as soon as they appear in that low-efficiency midrange shot zone.

Unfortunately, many of the teams that the Wolves will be playing against this season employ some semblance of “small ball” tactics — the Golden State Warriors being the best at this from that crop of teams — that are designed to collect efficient shots from outside the very 10–24 feet zone this Wolves team works so hard to protect defensively. This is why on shots beyond 24+ feet and inside 10 feet, the Wolves appear near the bottom of the defensive efficiency stats.

This is where Kevin Garnett’s veteran leadership will come in handy. If the team is already good enough at protecting that 10 to 24 feet area already, they can work even harder to expand that range even further so they don’t get annihilated by deep 3’s and near-the-basket 2’s. It will take some work for all involved parties (KG, Sam Mitchell, and the Wolves’s young talent), but as Garnett has said previously, “Anything is possiblllllleeeeeeeeee!!!!”
