The Hope of the North

The Toronto Raptors carry the hopes and dreams of not only Toronto, but also Canada

by Viraj Patel

Share Tweet

Conceivably, Dwane Casey’s Toronto Raptors should not have any obstacles standing in their path as they forage forward in their quest to clinch the Atlantic division’s crown for this 2015–2016 NBA season. Just over 30 games into this season, the divisional race has already turned into only a three-horse space as the Brooklyn Nets (9–23 record) and the Philadelphia 76ers (3–31) bowed out early —  without much shame — and began vying for the Ben Simmons Sweepstakes.

Brad Stevens’s Boston Celtics team sits in second in the division with an impressive 18–14 record; however, despite their defensive acumen and Stevens’s coaching abilities, the team has gone a subpar 5–5 in the last ten games, with their most recent “L” coming against Kobe Bryant’s 6–27 Los Angeles Lakers. Phil Jackson’s New York Knicks remain three games below .500, and while tanking doesn’t seem to be on the docket just yet, the team doesn’t exactly look equipped to put together a healthy winning streak any time soon, either.

Without much question, that division is Toronto’s for the taking, barring a sudden, prolonged uptick in Boston’s performances. While I’m sure Canada will be pleased with the Raptors clinching their third straight divisional crown, the club’s seeding heading into the postseason will be what’s most important. Since the NBA did away with the antiquated and disastrous seeding methods of the past this summer and introduced a seeding system based on the teams’ regular season record, Toronto’s exertions towards earning a division title no longer guarantees a top four seed. And in an Eastern Conference that’s inching towards parity — only four wins separate the top team (Cleveland, 21–9) and the tenth team (Detroit, 17–15) — and performance, the pressure to earn the proper seed, not just the best seed will be on Dwane Casey’s team.

All of this postseason planning matters because despite Toronto’s excellent regular season records in the past few seasons, their postseason outings leave much to be desired. After clinching the fourth seed last season, the Raptors were mercilessly ousted by John Wall’s Washington Wizards, who came through the 416 area code with broomsticks in hand. The year before that (the 2013–2014 season), the Raptors went into the playoffs with the third seed and were knocked out by the Brooklyn Nets in seven games.

These lackluster postseason performances from the Raptors stand in dissonance compared to the flamboyant outputs of their regular seasons. That sense of opulence and larger-than-life aura surrounding the Raptors is backed by two main forces converging in unified harmony: All of Canada and Mr. Hotline Bling himself, the rapper Drake. GM Masai Ujiri’s promise in 2013 to “bring the best basketball” to Toronto edges closer to being fulfilled each season. In February 2016, Drake, Ujiri, and the Raptors will open the city to the NBA’s elite as the All-Star game festivities get under way.

Since that wildly successful 2013–2014 season, the Raptors have appeared in the top ten of the league’s attendance charts. This year, the crowds in Toronto have only increased as the team boasts the fourth-best attendance numbers in the league. The team’s star players, like Kyle Lowry, DeMar DeRozan, Jonas Valančiūnas, and DeMarre Carroll, have all performed above their expectations. On any given night, that foursome, plus veteran forward Luis Scola, is responsible for putting up around 77.6 points. The bench runs deep, too, with the likes of Cory Joseph, Terrence Ross, Bismack Biyombo, James Johnson, and Patrick Patterson collectively contributing close to 33 additional points.

Despite everyone’s important contributions, it’s not difficult to deduce that this is clearly a team led by Kyle Lowry and DeMar DeRozan. After causing a media storm in the preseason due to his noticeable weight loss, Kyle Lowry successfully translated that offseason work into on-court efficiency. As of Thursday morning, the 29-year-old Lowry sports an impressive clip of 20.6 PPG, 6.2 APG, and 4.8 RPG. His running mate, DeRozan, boasts an equally astounding stat line of 22.9 PPG, 4.2 APG, and 4.5 RPG.

What’s even more sublime than those gaudy stats is the fact that Lowry and DeRozan have started all 33 games this season, a feat matched by only the indefatigable Luis Scola. Additionally, Lowry’s quick hands have resulted in 2.21 steals per game, a mark bettered by only the hyper-aggressive Russell Westbrook (2.50 SPG) and the quick-thinking Ricky Rubio (2.31 SPG). But his 98 turnovers (12th most in the league), combined with the 82 collected by DeRozan (20th most), signify the fact that there’s plenty to be improved.

On the defensive end, Lowry has the highest percentage on field goals defended (66.7%) among backcourt players, which means opposition players score on him every two out of three attempts. DeRozan is also culpable in this as he allows opposition players to score on him 61.2% of the time. Big man Jonas Valančiūnas is no better at this, either, as he posts the second-highest field goals defended percentage at 57.4% among the league’s big men. What’s even more confusing as we parse through these shoddy individual defensive numbers is that, as a team, the Raptors are tied for fifth (with OKC) in terms of the lowest defended field goal percentage. How is this possible when the team’s best three players play listless defense?

That superb overall defensive standing in Toronto comes from guys like Luis Scola (52.2% opposition field goal percentage), Cory Joseph (45.6%), Bismack Biyombo (46.1%), Patrick Patterson (43.4%), and James Johnson (38.9%), who have all selflessly taken up the defensive duties on this team, so that the team’s three stars can focus on the offensive end. Dwane Casey’s magic formula has worked thus far since Lowry, DeRozan, and Valančiūnas have all excelled offensively, but with the pressures of the postseason, if one of those studs misfire, the entire balance of the team can be easily destroyed.

What is also apparent is that as opponents shoot from outside ten feet against the Raptors, their conversion rates are unbelievably good since that’s the precarious and porous Lowry-DeRozan zone of Toronto’s defense. Inside ten feet, the Raptors have the league’s best defense, bar none. This trend replicates itself since Toronto falls within the bottom ten of the league’s worst 3-pt defending teams, but simultaneously finds itself perched in the two-spot (behind Memphis) as the NBA’s best 2-pt defending team.

When it comes to playing crunch-time defense, count Kyle Lowry and DeMar DeRozan out. But when it comes to playing crunch-time offense? Kyle and DeMar are the very best at what they do. What they can’t do is play catch-and-shoot basketball, something that the San Antonio Spurs and the Golden State Warriors have perfected over the years. Collectively, the Raptors check in in the bottom third in the league when it comes to playing catch-and-shoot. In fact, the Raptors prefer to play a more free-flowing, isolation style as Lowry, Cory Joesph, and DeRozan all appear in the top 50 in terms of field goals made when taking 7+ dribbles.

So this is what it’s come to, right? Toronto is the team that’s hellbent on shedding the postseason demons of its past while posting outstanding regular season results. Although this results in some spectacular basketball, as Dwane Casey heads into the twilight of his three-year contract, his coaching methods and style will be put under the national microscope that Canada has concocted. If the Raptors fail, then the ghosts of Casey’s past — his alleged inability to juggle lineups, some clock management gaffes, and the improper use of late-game fouling — will come back to haunt him. If he somehow escapes unscathed through the first few rounds of the playoffs next summer, his prize will most assuredly be a showdown with the 31-year-old King James and the Cleveland Cavaliers.

Hardly a proper reward for such a dazzling and promising team operating from the heart of our Northern neighbors.
