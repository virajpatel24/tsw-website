The Pride of Boston

The Celtics’ 13–9 start to the NBA season has captivated New England’s attention; however, will it continue?

by Viraj Patel

Share Tweet

It seems so long ago that the New England Patriots were a perfect 10–0 NFL club. After their thrilling 20–13 victory over the Rex Ryan-led Buffalo Bills on November 23rd (that allowed the Patriots to go 10–0), Tom Brady’s team has gone on to suffer two straight defeats in the past two weeks. But while the Patriots were busy wreaking havoc in the AFC East on that autumnal November day, another Boston professional sports team was going relatively unnoticed (at least on the national spectrum). In fact, Brad Stevens’s Celtics side was just out of the top eight in the Eastern Conference at the time, thanks to their listless 7–6 record.

Fast forward to today and you’ll see a Celtics team that plays free-flowing basketball that’s led to a healthy 13–9 record. In fact, while that record places them seventh in the East, the parity of the East is so triumphant that only one solitary win separates the Celtics and the top-seeded 14–7 Cleveland Cavaliers. That happy scenario lies in stark contrast to the one currently unfolding in Foxborough. As the Patriots have gone on their two-game skid, the Celtics have been scorching opponents with their +5.4 point differential, which checks in at fourth-highest in the league after the West’s top three sides: the Golden State Warriors (23–0 record, +14.6 differential), the San Antonio Spurs (18–5 record, +11.2), and the Oklahoma City Thunder (14–8 record, +7.1).

And from that terrifying Western triumvirate, those perfect Golden State Warriors have garnered every New Englander’s attention going into this weekend. The 23–0 Warriors, led by none other than the Baby-faced Assassin, Stephen Curry, himself, will descend upon the TD Garden on Friday night as they seek to continue their historic, dominant escapades through the league’s finest teams. After their convincing 23rd win on Tuesday night in Indianapolis, the Warriors are headed into this sixth game of their seven-game road trip with a squad that’s operating somewhat below full strength. Harrison Barnes is currently sidelined with a niggling ankle injury and Klay Thompson, who suffered a sprained ankle during the Pacers game, is listed as a game-time decision for tonight’s match-up against the C’s.

Given the shortage in rotation players and the fact that the Celtics are rounding into tip-top shape heading into this game, this is the prevalent question being whispered in every corner of the NBA kingdom: Will the upstart Celtics upset a weakened Warriors squad?

Since the potent Celtics offense (103.2 PPG) is still no match for the juggernaut that the Warriors have offensively (115.8 PPG), the spotlight will be centered around Boston’s defensive abilities. The C’s host the league’s fourth-best defense when it comes to defending 3’s and, to top it all off, the team also checks in at seventh-best when they’re defending shots taken less than six feet away from the rim.

Coincidentally, these defensive strengths are exactly what come in handy when facing the league’s best 3-pt shooting rotation: The Curry-Klay-Rush-Draymond-Bogut rotation that the Dubs have been fielding during Harrison Barnes’s extended stay on the injury list has been lethal from the 3-pt arc (4.4 3’s made/10.4 3’s attempted). That lethality comes with its tragic flaw: turnovers. When Golden State uses that small-ball rotation, the team coughs up an inordinately high 4.6 turnovers per game. A rabid Celtics defense known for its tenacity and desire in racking up steals and forcing opposition turnovers will prove to be a tough barrier for the turnover-prone Dubs rotation to overcome.

Sure, the Warriors are no scrubs on the defensive end, either. But their late dismantling against the Pacers on Tuesday night — the Dubs held a 111–83 lead heading into the 4th quarter and nearly gave it all up until Steph Curry returned and put the game out of reach — showed that despite their otherworldly exploits on the hardwood this season, the team is showing clear signs of fatigue. With key stars like Harrison Barnes and Klay Thompson suffering untimely injuries, Golden State’s lean, mean machine may be nearing its final churns.

With a Christmas Day battle against King James’s Cavaliers looming, the Warriors should be treating this Celtics game tonight as a mere warm-up. But the stats and basic intuition overwhelmingly show that this Celtics team is not to be underestimated. Their inspired 105–100 victory on Wednesday night at the TD Garden against Chicago showed that the team shows up to play when the 4th quarter arrives, no matter the opposition.

Can Brad Stevens’s men find a way to knock out the weary defending champions? That question will answer itself tonight. But even if the C’s fail, they’ll have laid down the hammer on a Warriors team that hasn’t been truly tested since their close encounter with losing on November 5th when the Clippers came to the Bay hell-bent on leaving with a victory. But in narrowly failing, the Clips left a template for other teams to follow: Shoot lights out, make Stephen Curry uncomfortable (although that’s harder said than done), and defend with voracious hunger when the Warriors go to that lethal, but turnover-prone small-ball lineup.

Accordingly, if there’s another team that can ask questions of the Warriors before King James’s Christmas clash in a few weeks, it’s these Boston Celtics. They have Jared Sullinger’s otherworldly rebounding skills, Kelly Olynyk’s ability to take bigs away from the rim, Isaiah Thomas’s (21.0 PPG) scoring skills, Avery Bradley’s defense, and Amir Johnson’s rim protection. On paper, the Celtics are definitely capable of asking questions.

But in reality, let’s hope Stephen Curry’s raining 3’s don’t wash out the interview.
