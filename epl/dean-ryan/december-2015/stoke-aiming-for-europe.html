Stoke Aiming for Europe

The Potters dare to dream after consecutive victories over Manchester United and Everton

by Ryan Dean

Share Tweet

“You never know with football. The Premier League this season is so all over the place, some teams are really down and some are really up like Leicester [City]. Anything can happen in this league.”

— Xherdan Shaqiri, when asked by The Daily Star on the possibility of Stoke qualifying for the Champions League next season

And so it has come to this. The craziness of the Premier League this season has somehow made us talk about Stoke City - a club that’s a perennial mid-table dweller of the league - representing England in the UEFA Champions League for next season. It would be a mute point if this talk had occurred in a normal Premier League season like in the past, but these conversations are really now happening in the current 2015-16 season, where apparently anything and everything is possible. Are the high marks given to Stoke’s success premature? Yes. Ambitious? Yes. Will Stoke really make good on these sky-high projections? No.

Stoke are, of course, 10th in the Premier League currently, having accumulated 29 points off of their eight wins, five draws, and six losses. However, only two points separate them from the last European spot in fifth, which is currently occupied by Alan Pardew’s Crystal Palace. Their distance with the final Champions League spot in fourth, currently occupied by Tottenham, is a sizeable six points. While talk of qualifying for the Champions League is fantasy at best, it is safe to say that the top four are a lock, with Manchester United primed to be the other challenger if they can get a new manager. If that’s the case, then the Potters’ desire for European football, through the league standing, will surely be stunted.

So, what prompted this kind of egregious talk in the first place? Their consecutive victories over Manchester United and Everton in the space of just three days. Those conquests are very impressive indeed, especially that last-gasp win at Goodison Park. To put things into perspective, only Stoke and Tottenham have managed to maintain a perfect winning record over the cramped double fixtures, whereas table toppers Arsenal and Leicester only managed a win and a loss over that dreaded period. While Stoke won’t be challenging for the title, their achievement over the congested period certainly helped put the idea in everybody’s head at the Britannia regarding the possibility of competing for the prestigious Champions League spot.

The man responsible for Stoke daring to even dream of a Champions League spot is, of course, their manager, Mark Hughes. When Mr. Hughes took over the club for the 2013-14 season, he started work on an ambitious project, ridding the club of their reputation as an ultra-defensive behemoth - that simultaneously punted the ball upfield any chance they got - into a side that’s more easy on the eyes. This is the Stoke side that we know today. Gone are the days of a backline consisting of four center backs or relying on Rory Delap’s long throw-ins. If you want to see the Stoke of old, you only need to look at the Hawthorns, home of West Brom, where their former boss, Tony Pulis, is currently in charge.

Now, fans flocking to the Britannia can bear witness to the dribbling skills of players such as Xherdan Shaqiri, Bojan Krkić, Marko Arnautović, and Ibrahim Afellay. The Potters now have proper full backs at their disposal that they can deploy to create more attacking space in the likes of Erik Pieters and Glen Johnson. And, of course, the next great England goalkeeping hope, Jack Butland, also plies his trade for the Potters. All of these players converging at Stoke would’ve been an unthinkable proposition only a few years back, yet here they are doing their best. Most of them were discarded by major clubs in Europe, but they are now flourishing bit by bit, each having a point to prove. With their confidences restored, the Potters are the ones reaping the rewards.

It’s not as if the defense has been compromised with the recent attacking additions at the club, either. They are the fifth-best club defensively in the league, with only a meager 19 goals conceded, sitting behind Tottenham, Palace, Manchester United, and Arsenal. Ryan Shawcross is still the heart of the backline for Stoke, now partnered by the very reliable Philipp Wollscheid. The double pivot in midfield of Glenn Whelan and Geoff Cameron gives the defense extra protection, allowing the full backs to assist in the attack. Discounting the three goals let in against Everton this past Monday, the Potters’ defense is still the foundation upon which everything is built around.

While Champions League qualification is a bit too ambitious for Stoke, they can still improve upon their achievements from the previous two seasons. In Mark Hughes’s first season at the club, the 2013-14 season, they finished in 9th place with 50 points, their best-ever finish in the league since the 1974-75 season. In the 2014-15 season, they repeated their ninth place finish, only this time with a higher points tally (54 points). As mentioned above, they are currently in tenth place. So, while a third consecutive ninth place finish wouldn’t be so bad, they would do well to take advantage of the volatility in the English Premier League this season.

Entering 2016, Stoke still stand an outside chance of grabbing fifth place and qualifying for the Europa League, if marginal gains from the past two seasons are taken into account. There’s also the League Cup to look forward to, with only Jürgen Klopp’s Liverpool standing in their way in the two-legged semi-finals. If they get past the Reds, either Manchester City or Everton will await them in the final. Well, what do you know, another top ten finish with the probability of qualifying for Europe and winning a domestic cup? It’s a good time to be a Potters fan. Not bad Stoke, not bad.
