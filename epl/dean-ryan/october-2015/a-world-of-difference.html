A World of Difference

The most anticipated clash of the weekend ended in a dull 0-0 draw

by Ryan Dean

Share Tweet

I had a choice. Not in the magnitude of “Sophie’s Choice,” but I still had one. I had a choice between Old Trafford or Vitality Stadium. A sane soccer fan would definitely choose the former as it would be a theater showcasing the famed Manchester Derby, and choose the former I did. A choice that I made from the comfort of my own home. Maybe it’s the comfortness of it all that drove me to make that decision. Who wouldn’t want to watch the Manchester derby in this day and age? “The richest Manchester derby ever,” they say. Turns out it was a very bad choice on my part and, I’m sure millions of others across the globe feel the same way. That’s 90 plus minutes of my life down the drain. Precious minutes that I can’t get back.

Ever.

As you may well know, Manchester United played to a sleep-inducing 0-0 draw with Manchester City at Old Trafford. Now, I can forgive a 0-0 draw if it’s filled with end-to-end drama or exquisite goalscoring chances here and there; however, this Manchester derby was anything but dramatic and exquisite. Only two shots on target were mustered - one from either side - with both coming in the 82nd and 83rd minute, respectively. One was a tame shot from Jesús Navas while the other one was from Jesse Lingard, who did well to get on the end of Anthony Martial’s lofted pass only to see the resulting shot attempt hit the bar instead. All in all, there were six shots fired each, three were blocked, two were off-target, and the aforementioned on-target Lingard shot that smacked off the post, respectively. To put this in even grander perspective: Lord Louis’ Red Devils did not have a shot at all in the first half.

“I said at half-time that we can also shoot from outside the box, you don’t have to wait for the 16-metre area. That was my remark also, I thought ‘You can shoot, why don’t you shoot?’”

— Louis van Gaal, on United’s failure to shoot at goal in the first half

To describe the match as a tactical battle is a bit of an overstatement. At best, it’s a futile attempt to gloss over the dourness of it all. For City, it is understandable to call it tactical, since they are missing talismans in Sergio Agüero and David Silva, individuals who can turn the tide of the match in one magical moment. This is further proven by Manuel Pellegrini substituting the haphazard Yaya Touré with Martín Demichelis, a central defender by trade, in the 76th-minute to cement the draw. For United, however, seeing that they were operating at full-strength, it is simply unforgivable, considering their failure to even threaten City’s defense in the first half. Still, both sides’ defenses deserve much praise for their astuteness and solidity, even though their offenses left the viewing audience wanting.

Over at the Vitality Stadium, home of Bournemouth, a lot of interesting storylines were made. Matt Ritchie scored after just 49 seconds for the Cherries, resulting in the fastest goal in the Premier League this season. However, Harry Kane was revitalized from the start and scored a hat-trick, leading Spurs to a smashing 5-1 win after the early goal from Ritchie. Kane’s hat-trick was his second hat-trick in the Premier League, becoming the 5th youngest to score a second triple at only 22 years of age, behind Michael Owen (18), Robbie Fowler (19), Chris Sutton (21) and Yakubu (21). Also, the renaissance of Moussa Dembélé continues with another exceptional performance in midfield for Spurs. He even scored a goal this time around - along with Erik Lamela also chipping in a goal - to finish the 5-1 demolition job.

Oh, how I envy those who tuned in to this match. There was lots going on. Goal in the opening minute? Check. Out-of-form striker finally scored (with a hat-trick to boot)? Check. Errors by the goalkeeper (in this case, Bournemouth’s goalkeeper Artur Boruc)? Discount double check! I understand that to compare this match with the Manchester derby isn’t fair at all considering the level of competition, but, in this one anomaly of a match, the Spurs/Bournemouth match offered exactly what the derby didn’t. And, it’s just a better experience to watch Harry Kane’s perseverance throughout his goal drought rewarded with a hat-trick than watching Wayne “Wazza” Rooney flop around without purpose on the Old Trafford pitch. It’s better to watch a creative attacking midfielder in action in the form of Christian Eriksen than watching a dumbfounded Kevin de Bruyne and a dumber-founded Juan Mata. Granted, on most occasions, both United’s and City’s star players will shine brighter than others, but this past Sunday, for whatever reason, that simply wasn’t the case.

The next time the Manchester derby will be contested will be on the 19th of March 2016 at the Etihad Stadium. By that time, a whole legion of fans on both sides of the divide and neutral fans alike will hope for much more. And I think that match should regress to the norm, which is a strong possibility seeing that the stakes will be much higher by the time we reach March. We might even see some new players strutting their stuff (thanks to the usual shake-ups caused by the January transfer window) and, perhaps, even a new manager on the touchline. Till then, let’s just enjoy the weeks to come, starting with the next big game on the horizon, which will have Chelsea hosting Liverpool at Stamford Bridge this Saturday in a do-or-die battle for the embattled José Mourinho.

Ryan Dean
