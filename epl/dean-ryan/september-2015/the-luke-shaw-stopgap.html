The Luke Shaw Stopgap

With Luke Shaw out for the season, let's examine who’ll fill in the void left at both Manchester United and England.

by Ryan Dean

Share Tweet

While all the talk at St. Mary’s was about Manchester United’s 3-2 victory over Southampton and the excellence of Anthony Martial, I couldn’t help but notice the missing, looming figure of a certain Luke Shaw on the left hand side of United’s defense. Luke Shaw, of course, suffered a double leg fracture in the Champions League match against PSV last week, and question marks quickly descended on United regarding who will replace him on the left hand side of the defense.

It was down to a choice between Marcos Rojo and Daley Blind to start at left-back, with Ashley Young being a wildcard of sorts to fill that spot. In the match against Southampton, all questions were answered as Louis van Gaal opted for Marcos Rojo at the position. Given the circumstances, it was a rather logical move on the part of the Dutchman as Daley Blind has formed somewhat of a blossoming partnership with Chris Smalling at the heart of the defense. But, did it really work? Let’s delve into the conundrum facing Louis van Gaal.

Marcos Rojo

The obvious choice to fill the vacant left-back spot. He is a starter there for Argentina, so his selection should be a no-brainer. If he can replicate his performances in Argentina colors for the Red Devils, Luke Shaw might have to fight for his place once he’s fit again many months from now. Seriously, when Rojo’s playing for Argentina, he is superb. He is a solid presence on the left-hand side for La Albiceleste defense, while backing up the attack in his forays forward. With Shaw out for a huge amount of time, now will be the perfect time for Rojo to show what he can produce for the Red Devils.

Daley Blind

Blind started out as a left-back at Ajax before progressing into a defensive midfield role for both club and country. However, at Manchester United, his versatility has proven to be a headache for Louis van Gaal, who was, ironically, his coach at the 2014 FIFA World Cup for the Netherlands. This season, he has transitioned into a centre-back, partnering with Chris Smalling since the start of the season. Disrupting this budding partnership will destabilize the balance of the defense, although more question marks will hover when out-and-out defender Phil Jones returns. But, right now, the Dutch international should stay where he’s at, and that is in the heart of United’s defense.

Ashley Young

For the life of me, I can’t even figure out why he is even in the contention for the left-back position. A faction of fans pointed out that he occupied the position earlier last season but let’s not get carried away. The ‘left-back’ position he was purportedly occupying early last season was actually a hybrid wing-back kind of role. Louis van Gaal was tinkering with his formation and, at the time, it was a 3-4-3 or a 3-5-2. This season, van Gaal has opted for the 4-2-3-1 since the beginning of the season. The defense is now a more orthodox four man backline. Ashley Young simply doesn’t have the defensive nous to perform as a fully fledged left-back. He is simply more attack-minded and should be plying his trade further forward, not in defense.

Jose Gaya

A more outrageous option is to buy another left-back when the transfer window reopens in January. What a way to demoralize the current available left-back options at the club. Jose Gaya, currently at Valencia, has been making the rounds over the weekend, being reported to be a transfer target of Chelsea and, you guessed it, Manchester United. He is, without a doubt, one of the best left-backs around and the early transfer interests surrounding him only solidify his rising status. However, United would do well to stick with the current options available in the team. Buying another LB would send the wrong message to the likes of Rojo and Shaw.

England National Team

So, what of the England national team? Luke Shaw looked primed to usurp Leighton Baines as the starting left-back for England until his freak injury. Since Baines himself was unavailable due to injury, the starting eleven position was Shaw’s for the taking. However, Baines is set to return next month and appears ready to regain and solidify his place in the English side. Even if Baines were to be out even longer than expected, England is well stocked in left-back options heading into Euro 2016.

Leighton Baines

Mr. Baines was the heir apparent to the left-back position left vacant by Ashley Cole. His injury has allowed Luke Shaw, in recent months, to stake a claim for the left-back position. Now that Shaw himself is out, and Baines himself is returning soon, he can solidify his place for England and the Euros all and for once. He is by far the best choice for the left-back position and looks set to keep it until the 2018 World Cup, barring any mishaps.

Kieran Gibbs

With Luke Shaw out for the season (and maybe the Euros as well), Arsenal’s Kieran Gibbs also has the chance to make it into the England team as a formidable backup to Leighton Baines. Solid in defense and attack, he has all the makings of the modern day fullback. If only he could get some more match experience at Arsenal, it will solidify his case for a place in the England squad. Nacho Monreal has been the starting left-back at Arsenal for a while now, and this will dent Gibbs’ hopes of being a consistent figure in the Three Lions’ Euro 2016 preparations.

Danny Rose

Like his last name suggests, Danny Rose has bloomed into a beautiful ‘Rose’ right before our very eyes. He’s the one that looks set to gain the most from Shaw’s injury setback and Gibbs’ inactivity. Rose is currently rediscovering his fitness and form after returning from injury and will look to take back his starting left-back spot at Spurs from Ben Davies. Once the Spurs aspect of his resurgence is complete, he will be well on his way to sneaking into contention for the backup LB slot behind Baines.

So, there you have it: the Luke Shaw stop-gap. Luke Shaw’s role at Manchester United and England has been that his absence presents a myriad of selection problems for Louis van Gaal and Roy Hodgson, respectively. No player deserves to suffer the atrocious (and potentially career-threatening) injury he suffered against PSV. And, in due time, the young Shaw will come back better than ever and continue his never-ending quest to seek out his true potential as a world class left-back.
