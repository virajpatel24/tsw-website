English Premier League Recap - Matchday 25

A stalemate at Stamford Bridge leaves Chelsea in euphoria and Manchester United demoralized

by Viraj Patel

Share Tweet

In a tumultuous week that exposed the very real and raw insecurities of Louis van Gaal, as the media rounded on the 64-year-old Dutchman in regards to his shaky job security at Old Trafford, it became evident that this past Sunday’s match against Guus Hiddink’s Chelsea squad didn’t exactly deserve the full attention it should have from the Notorious LvG. How else can United’s head honcho explain away the inexplicable decision to insert Marouane Fellaini into the Starting XI - in the place of the sturdy, lean, and reliable Morgan Schneiderlin - when nearly every statistical measure claims that United become Aston Villa 2.0 when Fellaini is playing?

But more to the point: How can van Gaal possibly put two-and-two together and deem it wise to send a misfiring Memphis “Haven’t-scored-since-November-21-2015-against-Watford” Depay in place of the lone United goalscorer Jesse Lingard in the 87th minute? Even if the Lingard substitution was imminent, it still doesn’t excuse van Gaal from choosing Depay in the place of more defensive-minded candidates, like Gullermo Varela, Paddy McNair, and Andreas Pereira. It’s as simple as it sounds, when push came to shove, that dubious Depay-for-Lingard substitution reeked of haphazard decision-making that simply won’t win Premier League games.

To van Gaal’s credit, he has given United’s young talent, including Depay, many chances to display their talents during this campaign. Against Chelsea at Stamford Bridge this past weekend, he gave starts to Jesse Lingard and Cameron Borthwick-Jackson when he could have opted for Memphis Depay, or brought in replacements via the (now-closed) January transfer window. And van Gaal’s risky maneuver paid off in the end, since Lingard and Borthwick-Jackson changed the dynamic of the United attack with their clever interplay and intricate movements in-and-out of the penalty area. Alongside David de Gea, who remained relatively perfect in United’s goal until Diego Costa came asking questions in the 91st minute, Lingard and Borthwick-Jackson were easily United’s leading contenders for Man of the Match honors.

While Lingard wreaked havoc throughout the fixture, it was easy to see that United’s defense – which has only conceded 22 goals this 2015-16 season (tied with Arsenal for the second-fewest in the Premiership) – would hold up capably against the nonexistent Chelsea attacks in the second half, especially with Eden Hazard and Pedro rooted to the bench. Since the lone attacker in Diego Costa wasn’t enough of a threat with de Gea warding off each and every Chelsea shot, if van Gaal had opted for the Morgan Schneiderlin-Michael Carrick tandem in the heart of the Red Devils midfield right from the start, the game may have unfolded a bit differently, since United would have retained the majority of possession in the second-half, too. And this is exactly where van Gaal’s opposite, Guus Hiddink, outsmarted him: Hiddink deployed the tried-and-tested combo of John Obi Mikel and Nemanja Matić right from the beginning. In the 67 minutes they played together (before Matić was substituted off for Pedro), the duo helped rattle the United attacks, delivered timely challenges, and, most importantly, retained possession, which confounded van Gaal’s men throughout the duration of their outing.

In fact, according to The Guardian’s match report, Chelsea retained 51% of the possession, which is the second straight match that Manchester United’s opposition has maintained the brunt of the possession (Stoke had 56% possession en route to a 3-0 loss to United six days ago). But these downward trends in possession percentages underlie a philosophical evolution occurring with the Red Devils: They are shifting to a formation that leads to more width in key areas of the pitch.

“That said, the width wasn’t provided in the conventional manner: Van Gaal fielded no touchline-hugging wingers, and instead fielded players who drifted inside into central positions. Jesse Lingard acted as an extra central midfielder at times, while Anthony Martial twice went close having cut inside from the left before shooting powerfully with right foot, the Frenchman’s trademark move. The width came from deep. Full-back duo Cameron Borthwick-Jackson and Matteo Darmian were both excellent, taking up aggressive starting positions and timing their overlaps at the perfect moments.”

— Michael Cox, The Guardian

On the Chelsea end, both Oscar and Willian swerved and verved their way into the Manchester United penalty area; however, their efforts did not result in many successful chances on goal, thanks to David de Gea’s brilliant goalkeeping and the uncompromising discipline and structure of the United defense, buttressed by Daley Blind and “Mike” Smalling. Oftentimes, it seemed as if Oscar and Willian were constantly seeking out open areas in the middle of the pitch, rather than embracing the wide-open green grass already available to them due to Darmian’s and Borthwick-Jackson’s forward forays.

This is where the pivotal foresight of Hiddink in inserting lethal, wide-area playmakers like Eden Hazard and Pedro came in handy, as the Belgian and Spanish internationals finally began to expose the acres of space left on the United flanks. The dynamic duo’s looping runs began to allow Cesc Fàbregas and Willian, who was now in a central role, to operate with more freedom when it came to unleashing perfectly-weighted through balls for Diego Costa to rocket past de Gea. With Obi Mikel still shepherding the midfield and the Terry-Cahill English alliance not totally floundering, Chelsea was able to retain more of the possession and, in due time, Fantastic Fabs threaded the needle through a clogged United defense to find Costa, who took a few nifty, well-calculated steps past David de Gea to slide in the deciding equalizer. In one fell swoop, all that Manchester United had worked to tirelessly accrue in the past ninety minutes disappeared faster than a Panthers possession during a Von Miller strip sack.

Football is a funny sport at the end of the day. There’s very few things in life that simply disappear faster than a 1-0 lead during a football match. Poof! And it’s gone before you even knew it. Just like its American counterpart, it’s a game of inches. That final Fàbregas through ball had to slide its way through countless United and Chelsea boots before it finally found its way to an opportunistic Diego Costa. Who really knows if the Depay substitution was to blame, or if the United defense simply got caught snoozing on a conspicuously hidden, match-deciding moment early into stoppage time? These are things that even the Notorious LvG can’t predict or account for, despite his 30-year-old managerial career. It happens.

Besides, as the great American philosopher and another Notorious (B.I.G., in this case) once said, “I love it when you call me Big Poppa.”
