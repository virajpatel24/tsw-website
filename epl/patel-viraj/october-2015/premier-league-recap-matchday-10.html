English Premier League Recap - Matchday 10

Arsenal win against Everton in a rainy, hazy night in London

by Viraj Patel

Share Tweet

In case you missed it, these are the “titles” currently being assigned to various Premier League managers after the tenth week of EPL matches (the first two titles are not my creations):

1. “Jürgen Flopp — Specialist-in-draws”

2. Garry Monk — “Very sharp or a total fish of a manager”

3. Louis van Gaal — Head of happiness at Manchester United

4. Slaven Bilić — Connoisseur of the perfect throw-in

5. José Mourinho — Guardian of the Relegation Zone Galaxy

6. Arsène Wenger — Head Professor of Arsenal University, the First of His Name, Caretaker of the Emirates, Shepherd of Scintillating Football, Resident Scrooge, Protector of the Arsenal Realm, and Destroyer of All Teams On This Planet (and the Neighboring Ones)

Personally, I felt most of these managers more than deserved the title they got given their performances with their respective clubs this season, although the Klopp one seems a bit hurried given he has only had three weeks to patch together a Liverpool side that’s only recently ripped off the Brendan Rodgers band-aid.

And the others are reasonable descriptions, if not comical. But the one that caught my eye was the last one featuring Arsenal boss and sexagenarian Arsène Wenger. Being an Arsenal fan can be simultaneously one of the world’s greatest joys and the world’s deepest pit of fear, sorrow, and frustration. In some years, you get to experience unbelievable seasons like 2003–2004 “Invincibles” season. But, in other years (of which there are quite a few), you are strapped and trapped in the front row of a pull-all-the-hair-out-of-your-head roller coaster season that somehow still ends up with Arsenal finishing in the top four.

These performances lie in stark contrast to some other EPL clubs, like Tottenham. You see, Spurs fans know that they’re destined for the Europa League and, so despite all of their kicking and screaming, they realize deep down that they’re perennially tethered to the fifth or sixth spot in the EPL table every season. An Arsenal supporter, on the other hand, knows that while his or her team isn’t a world-beater, it certainly isn’t a stinking pile of discarded trash either. It is that precarious “in between” that creates the unnecessary drama, upsetting losses, and unexpected wins.

Add that to the fact that despite having a reasonable transfer budget, Wenger and his Arsenal side don’t always field “the best team that money can buy” the way a Manchester City might thanks to Sheikh Mansour and his billions. Arsenal also play in one of the world’s toughest domestic leagues in the Premier League so their EPL and European campaigns are much more demanding and taxing than those of Bayern Munich or Juventus.

But all of those doubts and injustices were quieted last week as Arsenal defeated the mighty Bayern Munich 2–0 in North London mid-week. Then, building off of that momentum, the Gunners impressed in a thrilling 2–1 win over an Everton side that was coming off of a 3–0 mauling by Manchester United the previous weekend. To cap it all off, José Mourinho’s Chelsea side lost their fifth EPL match in ten games thanks to a sprightly 2–1 West Ham victory. But wait, it gets better.

After going down a goal after a Mauro Zárate strike, Nemanja Matić, who had already picked up a yellow earlier in the match, decided to aggressively ward off a Cheikhou Kouyaté dribble into the Chelsea left flank by pulling him back, which gave the referee on hand, Jonathan Moss, enough evidence to show the struggling Chelsea defensive midfielder his second yellow and the corresponding red card.

Not one to handle these types of controversial referee decisions with grace and poise, Mourinho went after Moss during half-time and got himself and two of his Chelsea assistants kicked out of the match. Clearly, Mourinho’s Blues team had seen enough for the day and after half-time, they silently clambered back into their relegation zone-shaped shell and waited out the impending loss. To add fuel to the smoldering Chelsea fire, the FA will now proceed with charges and fines against both Chelsea and Mourinho for their actions during the West Ham match.

Roman [Abramovich], please do this one thing for me and ignore those churlish Hammer fans who belted out their lusty chorus of “You’re Getting Sacked In The Morning.” Because let’s face it, watching Jose find a new way to melt down every week is too much fun. Long may he suffer!

— David Hirshey, ESPN FC, “Arsenal continue to impress as Chelsea and Jose Mourinho remain in freefall”

As Mourinho deals with the spontaneous combustion of Cesc Fàbregas and Matić, as Louis van Gaal contends with the inefficiencies of Memphis Depay and Wayne Rooney, and as Manuel Pellegrini figures out how to field a City side lacking Sergio Agüero and David Silva every week, it becomes clear that Arsène Wenger’s biggest tactical problem — Should he start Olivier Giroud or Theo Walcott at striker? — is not only causing this Arsenal side to not implode, but it is also somehow leading to great results both in Europe and in England these past few weeks.

The linchpin behind these superb Gunners results has been the German international Mesut Özil, who despite suffering the wrath of Arsenal fans for his lackadaisical defending and play at times in seasons past, has been quietly impressive all along. In fact, I came across this stat that blew me away. According to Mirror, Özil has dished out “21 assists in 57 Arsenal games,” which means he sets up a goal every 2.71 matches.

CHART COURTESY OF MIRROR

This feat elevates him above Premier League legends like Thierry Henry, David Silva, David Beckham, Cesc Fàbregas (who had 18 assists last EPL campaign with Chelsea), and Eric Cantona. Place Özil next to Santi Cazorla (who also appears on that list) and Francis Coquelin and Arsenal’s core midfield emanates. Having Alexis Sanchez, Aaron Ramsey, and Alex Oxlade-Chamberlain around to patrol the wings, while Theo Walcott and Olivier Giroud provide the goals is not a bad foundation for an Arsenal side keen on winning a few major trophies this season. And, as of last weekend, Arsenal are now tied with Manchester City at the top of the EPL table in terms of points. Somehow it still doesn’t feel like this Arsenal side has hit its true stride yet, although they’re clearly on that path.

Arsène Wenger’s stewardship of the Arsenal ship has been (mostly) thoroughly enjoyable this season, has afforded him the privilege of being called “The Head Professor of Arsenal University, the First of His Name, Caretaker of the Emirates, Shepherd of Scintillating Football, Resident Scrooge, Protector of the Arsenal Realm, and Destroyer of All Teams On This Planet (and the Neighboring Ones).” It’s a big responsibility and a few slight missteps in the coming weeks could mean the Arsenal fans can easily turn against him and lay siege upon his claim to the throne.

But there is one manager that has an even more glorious managerial résumé than Le Professeur. Of course, I am referring to the retired 73-year-old Manchester United manager Sir Alex Ferguson, who recently published his 1,492nd book called “Leading.” And it is from that book that Sir Alex’s greatest title descends:

There you have it: Sir Alex Ferguson — Fan of Spiderman, Beyoncé, and Lady Gaga.

As ESPN’s senior MLB writer Jayson Stark likes to say: whaddayaknow?
