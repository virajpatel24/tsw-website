English Premier League Recap - Matchday 8

The joys of being an Arsenal fan

by Viraj Patel

Share Tweet

In Manchester United superstar Wayne Rooney’s 2012 autobiography “Wayne Rooney: My Decade in the Premier League,” Captain Wazza describes the feeling of disillusionment that comes with becoming an individual that is constantly hounded by the fans, media, and the greater world: “It’s like I’m a waxwork from Madame Tussauds rather than a person.”

Well, unfortunately not much has changed for Rooney off the pitch as he’s an internationally recognized superstar. But, on Sunday afternoon, Rooney carried all of his exquisite Madame Tussauds waxwork abilities straight to the beautiful Emirates pitch as his top-of-the-table United side faced off against the joga bonito of Arsenal. From the opening whistle, Arsenal began making Rooney and Manchester United look more like the second coming of Chelsea (to be specific, this year’s Chelsea, who currently sit in 16th-place in the EPL table) than the side that had catapulted itself to first place in the table after seven matches.

In fact, as Arsenal fired shots among shots at David de Gea’s goal, I kept hoping the NBC Sports cameras would turn and point towards the Manchester United bench more often so that I could confirm that it was still Louis van Gaal, and not the newly-christened “specialist-in-failure” that is Chelsea boss José Mourinho, perched next to Ryan Giggs. Because, as you probably know by now, anything the Portuguese Mourinho comes into contact with transforms into a loser.

Don’t believe me? Well, his thoroughly disappointing Chelsea side this season would be my primary example. But, to those still unconvinced, I offer the following circumstantial evidence.

Remember when Arsenal went to Stamford Bridge on September 19th and the magnanimous human being that is José Mourinho took the higher road (for once) and shook his arch-nemesis Arsène Wenger’s hand pre-match? Well, while the media feted and fawned over the duo’s alleged “making up,” I immediately knew the travesty of what had just occurred: Wenger was bound to lose after that physical contact with the new “specialist in failure” Mourinho. And, ninety minutes later, a dejected Arsène Wenger stormed down the Stamford Bridge tunnel as his nine-man Gunners team lost 2–0.

I think that example should be more than enough to convince you of Mourinho’s cunning ways.

Thankfully, the Portuguese had his own set of problems to deal with over at Chelsea on Sunday afternoon given his side’s 3–1 loss at home to Southampton, so his influence was close to nil in the Arsenal-United offering. However, if Mourinho didn’t interfere with any of van Gaal’s decisions, then it really must be van Gaal making those decisions, right? Right.

The first mistake the Dutchman made, in my view, was choosing Michael Carrick over Morgan Schneiderlin in central midfield. Schneiderlin is an out-and-out defensive midfielder who would have helped out the central-defense pairing of Daley Blind and Chris Smalling, who were left to fend for themselves against the Arsenal counterattack all day Sunday. Carrick’s lack of speed and defensive acumen hurt United’s right flank throughout Sunday’s match. Bastian Schweinsteiger’s play wasn’t as deplorable as Carrick’s, but the German left plenty to be desired and also looked woefully ill-prepared for such a galvanized Arsenal side.

The second mistake van Gaal made also centered around the starting eleven. He, in a fit of unfathomable cluelessness, chose to start Ashley Young at the left back position when he is clearly the equivalent of a firecracker on the offensive end and a fireworks injury on the defensive end. Of course, van Gaal’s sleight of hand was forced since the team’s usual left backs Marcos Rojo and Luke Shaw were out due to injury. But Young’s inclusion at left back hurt the team time and time again as Mesut Özil, Theo Walcott, Héctor Bellerín, and others flooded Young’s flanks in the hopes of catching the winger-turned-left-back off-guard.

And catch him off-guard they did as Francis Coquelin stole the ball off of Memphis Depay in the 6th minute, passed it to Özil, who threaded it to Aaron Ramsey, who found Özil making the perfect defense-splitting run (made possible thanks to Ashley Young completely leaving the defense to fend for itself). Özil, no stranger to assist-making, found a cutting Alexis Sanchez, who flicked in the game’s first goal.

Had Young tracked Özil after he made the decisive pass to Ramsey instead of setting up camp against Bellerín, the United defense wouldn’t have utterly combusted. Things didn’t improve much for Young and the Red Devils since just 33 seconds later, Arsenal scored a second goal after a lung-bursting Theo Walcott-engineered attack that ended in an Özil goal.

In the 19th minute, Walcott was once again instrumental as he relayed a pass to Alexis, who tiptoed past the heaviest of the United defenses (Darmian and Smalling) to thunder in the game’s third goal. From then on, there was no doubt that Arsenal would see out the victory. Admittedly, the game in North London became such a snoozefest that I ended up tuning in to large swathes of the Bayern Munich-Borussia Dortmund match occurring at the same time.

Which brings us to Captain Wazza and his “Madame Tussauds waxwork” analogy from earlier. At various points during the match, he was seen shouting well-chosen English pleasantries towards his hapless teammates and kicking up a storm with the referees on hand. And, at other times, unless he was pummeling a speedy Arsenal winger to the ground in frustration or lackadaisically defending in his own half, Wazza was as effective as a waxwork statue.

And, apparently, as glorified as one, too.
