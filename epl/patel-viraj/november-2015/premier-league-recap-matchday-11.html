English Premier League Recap - Matchday 11

Chelsea keep floundering while Arsenal’s excellent run of form continues

by Viraj Patel

Share Tweet

After his side’s narrow 1–0 win over CSKA Moscow last night in the Champions League, Manchester United manager Louis van Gaal took center-stage at his routine post-match press conference. During the course of the proceedings, LvG accidentally referred to his central defender Chris Smalling as “Michael” Smalling. Of course, being a 64-year-old AARP member like LvG means you’re bound to forget a few things here and there, like the name of one of your defensive studs.

But one manager who has never forgotten the names of his players or those who’ve infringed upon his “Special” aura is Chelsea’s José Mourinho. The 52-year-old beleaguered Blues head honcho has been known to point a finger or two towards his dithering players and perfectly capable personnel. For starters, Mourinho, by name, publicly called out and berated two members of his medical staff — Dr. Eva Carneiro and Jon Fearn — for attending to an injured Eden Hazard during the first match of this EPL season.

Carneiro, who was unceremoniously dismissed from the Chelsea staff after that incident, will be formally suing the Portuguese for “constructive dismissal.” Over the course of these last eleven Premier League matches, the hot-headed Mourinho has even publicly called out his own players in Cesc Fàbregas, Nemanja Matić, John Terry, Branislav Ivanović, among others.

And the Portuguese’s abrasive behavior this season isn’t anything out of the ordinary. In fact, during his previous managerial stints, he had fallen out with star players like Juan Mata, Iker Casillas, Sergio Ramos, Pepe, William Gallas, Kevin De Bruyne, and Cristiano Ronaldo. And when he’s not busy deriding the players under his charge, Mourinho spends the majority of his working hours berating the match officials every time Chelsea suffer the slightest of injustices.

For instance, during half-time of the October 24th defeat at the hands of West Ham, Mourinho berated the officiating crew and the head referee, Jonathan Moss, which resulted in Mourinho and two of his assistants being relegated to watch from the stands. He’s even set to receive yet another warning and suspension from the FA for his deleterious actions. And last but not least, the Portuguese has gone after those very individuals who report his scathing remarks: Journalists.

Mourinho criticized journalists for not reporting on games fairly. Initially, he refused to answer questions, simply asking journalists whether they saw the game. Later, he offered us “a nice pair of glasses,” and suggested we needed “five percent of a personality,” to have the courage to say what happened.

— Michael Cox, ESPN FC, “Chelsea players fight for Mourinho but Blues boss losing his battle”

All of these insensitive outbursts from the Special One would’ve been forgiven or patched over had his Chelsea side continued on its scintillating form from the first-half of the 2014–2015 EPL campaign when they were virtually unbeatable. However, the world-beaters of Stamford Bridge have become the pitiable beaten. And the joyless turmoil that erupts from these calamitous circumstances has their Portuguese manager floundering and gaffing his way through the his few breathes of that polluted Chelsea air.

A manager that’s simply basking in the limelight of his side’s joyful, scintillating football is Arsenal’s Arsène Wenger. Monsieur Wenger has presided over one of Arsenal’s greatest performances in recent memory. It all started with that resounding 3–0 stomping of the aforementioned LvG’s Manchester United at the Emirates on October 4th. And, aside from a few blips here and there, the Gunners have proceeded to steamroll over every side they’ve faced since then.

In fact, what’s been the most impressive aspect of Arsenal’s performances during this euphoric winning streak is the fact that the Gunners have won with both their distinctive “maintain possession at all costs” philosophy and their explosive counterattacking verve. In that momentous 3–0 victory over United, Arsenal only had 38% possession, yet the Gunners still slotted three goals past David de Gea. During the October 20th Champions League tie against Bayern Munich, the Gunners only had the ball for 27% of the time, letting Pep Guardiola’s German side pass, pass, pass their way to a 2–0 loss.

But the possession percentages increased as Arsenal reverted to its tried-and-tested philosophy against Watford on October 17th. In that EPL match, Arsenal had 66.7% possession as they played to a 3–0 win at Vicarage Road. In the past, both Wenger and his team were generally caught out of sorts when it came to switching their core foundational styles of play like this from match-to-match, depending on the opponent. This year, however, Wenger has found ways to motivate his side to both defend aggressively during those Bayern Munich and Manchester United matches while also sitting back and dictating the pace when facing weaker foes, like Watford.

And tonight that blistering counterattacking philosophy will be put to the test once again as Arsenal takes on Bayern in Munich. Currently, Arsenal sit in fourth in their UCL group, but yet another victory over the Bavarian side will definitely put them in contention for the top place. All that remains to be seen is whether or not Le Professeur’s 90-minute lesson in counterattacking football goes according to plan at the Allianz Arena tonight or if it just disappears into the cloudy Munich night just like Arsenal’s Champions League aspirations.
