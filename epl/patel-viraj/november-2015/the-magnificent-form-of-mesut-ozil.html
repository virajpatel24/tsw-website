The Magnificent Form of Mesut Özil

The Arsenal and German player’s cache of spectacular performances in 2015 have impressed

by Viraj Patel

Share Tweet

On September 2nd, 2013, Arsenal F.C. sent out an announcement that sent shockwaves across England and the rest of the footballing world. The proclamation informed readers that Arsène Wenger’s team had (surprisingly) dipped into their cash reserves at Bank of Arsenal in order to whisk away a 24-year-old German midfielder named Mesut Özil from Florentino Pérez’s Real Madrid.

Suffice it to say, the sudden end to Özil’s popular run in the heart of Spain did not sit well with the likes of Cristiano Ronaldo and Álvaro Arbeloa. Even Real’s unlikeliest supporters had nothing but kind words to pass along:

“I was really surprised by Özil’s departure. He seemed to me the second-best player at Madrid after Cristiano.”

— Barcelona’s Cesc Fàbregas, who now plays for Chelsea F.C., as quoted by Laurie Whitwell of The Daily Mail

Despite the seemingly shocking turn of events at Madrid, the world’s richest club knew what it was doing all along. In fact, the ink had not even dried on Gareth Bale’s new £85.3 million (100 million euros) transfer deal (carried out on September 1st, 2013)—granting Bale a £300,000 per week salary over the next six years —as Madrid shipped off its beleaguered German maestro off to North London. But as the sprightly Wales forward brought a tsunami of unparalleled expectations and heightened scrutiny with him to Madrid, his counterpart — a wide-eyed German player with a liking for the tasty assist or two—arrived to an overjoyed North London (well, the red parts of North London were overjoyed; the navy parts were still stewing over the fact that they had just let their superstar Welshman hightail it to Real Madrid) that could almost sense its trophy-less seasons of the past melting away.

Two seasons and some change later, the Gunners’ midfield master has not disappointed. And he’s gone on to provide one scintillating assist after another with such fervor that even Cliff Paul and Sebastian Curry are looking into integrating him in their State Farm ads. While Mehmet Özil’s State Farm commercial potential is on the rise, his twin brother’s footballing career at Arsenal is also following a similar ascension of sorts.

In fact, amongst Arsenal attacking players, the German international boasts the best stat line of 2015 with his three goals and 16 assists across all competitions for both club and country. Only the indefatigable Alexis Sánchez has matched Özil’s production during this calendar year.

But the German international’s overall 2015 stats, while wildly impressive, may still prove to be too simplistic. This is why I explored the data a bit further by deducing the frequency (number of matches) at which these Arsenal attacking players have found the opponent’s goal or provided an assist that jump-started that process.

All stats courtesy of www.whoscored.com. The stats are representative of all 2015 matches played for club and country by these Arsenal players.

As expected, Mesut Özil places near the bottom of this new aggregated list, which means that he is Arsenal’s best attacking player. The German can be expected to score a goal himself or assist another player in doing so every 1.42 games in 2015. Saint Alexis has the next lowest rate at 1.63. Aaron Ramsey comes in last by going an astounding 7.67 matches between his next goal or assist.

Francis Coquelin is included in the list, but his frequency rating of 0.00 is actually a misnomer since he’s amassed zero goals and zero assists in 2015. Given Coquelin’s deep-lying midfielder role with the Gunners, his stunningly low offensive output makes sense and is, as expected, easily surmounted by his noteworthy defensive contributions (although the fantastic Mike L. Goodman of Grantland may disagree with me on that point).

When he rose to fame and fortune during the 2010 FIFA World Cup in South Africa, even Germans had no idea that their young “multi-kulti kicker” would turn into the phenom that he’s become today. Possessing breathtaking control and preternatural touch on the ball, as well as a transcendent eye for the defense-splitting through ball, the 27-year-old has played a pivotal role in bringing two FA Cups (2014, 2015) and two Community Shields (2014, 2015) to North London.

On the international side of things, Özil gained fame initially as Germany’s “first national player from an immigrant background to have made it internationally,” when he won his first cap for Germany in 2009. Now, with 69 more caps under his belt during the intervening years, the Gelsenkirchen native also has a FIFA World Cup trophy to his name, a feat that still escapes the living legends of world soccer like Cristiano Ronaldo and Lionel Messi (who came within a Mario Götze wonder strike of defeating Özil’s Germany in the 2014 World Cup final in Rio de Janiero).

While he missed out on Real Madrid’s Champions League-winning run in the 2013–14 season, Mesut Özil’s trophy cabinet is not lacking in its fair share of ornate inhabitants by any stretch of the imagination. And he’s played a pivotal role in convincing Arsène Wenger that his shortcomings and PTOs (Paid Time Off) on the defensive end from time-to-time are clearly overshadowed by his immense contributions in the attacking areas.

In dissecting his midfielder’s 2015 season, Wenger was quick to shed some insights on the types of thoughts that filter in endlessly through the mind of someone like Mesut Özil, who’s playing in the very middle of his prime:

“[Özil] is 27 now,” Wenger said. “It is the golden age of a football player, 27–31, 32, the age when you know your job, you realise suddenly that it is not only important to play, it is important to win as well. You go into that period thinking: ‘Finally’. Under 25 you think you can last forever. Once you get to 27 you realise it’s time to capitalise on what you have learned and efficiency takes a little bit over the playing mood.

— Arsène Wenger, as quoted by Jacob Steinberg in The Guardian

As the Premier League season dwindles into the busy winter period of matches, there are not too many 27-year-old midfielders in world football — aside from Sergio Busquets at Barcelona, Juan Mata at Manchester United, and Nemanja Matić at Chelsea (although his 2015 form has dipped enormously just like that of his London-based club) — that can swing the outcome of matches the way a Mesut Özil can for club and country.

Given this knowledge, it’s abundantly evident that the “multi-kulti kicker” from Gelsenkirchen will have a remarkable role to play both in his Arsenal side’s quest for more prestigious trophies and in his national side’s ambitions to win Euro 2016 this coming summer for the first time since 1996. Following that progression of the Özil-Arsenal-Germany triumvirate will be one of the most exciting storylines of the 2016 calendar year. And I, for one, cannot wait for that happy new year.
