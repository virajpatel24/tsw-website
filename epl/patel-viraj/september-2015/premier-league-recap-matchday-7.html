English Premier League Recap - Matchday 7

Louis van Gaal’s Happy Feeling

by Viraj Patel

Share Tweet

Louis van Gaal is one happy manager.

No, really!

Lucky Louis said it himself (multiple times) during his post-match interview. The source of the Dutchman’s newfound happiness is the combination of Manchester United’s convincing 3–0 victory against a lackluster Sunderland side at Old Trafford and the fact that the Red Devils, as a result, are now finally perched atop the Premier League table. If van Gaal is this happy about clinching first place, I can only wonder how some managers must feel as their respective sides slog through extremely turbulent campaigns.

One such manager feeling the Blues of his team’s deleterious decline was sourpuss-in-chief and Chelsea boss José Mourinho, who was found taking a few well-aimed jabs at his North London foe, Arsène Wenger, in the lead-up to Chelsea’s match at Newcastle. (Yes, I’m just as confused as you are as to why Mourinho would be talking about Arsenal and Wenger at a press conference about the impending Newcastle match. Talk about a “specialist in failure” at press conferences!)

Of course, given his side’s thoroughly deplorable performance at Newcastle, it would behoove the Portuguese to focus on his own team’s constant battles to avoid relegation instead of launching thinly-veiled jabs at Le Professeur. Also, given that his mind has been busy simultaneously processing different ways to erroneously handle team doctors and putting a lid on Diego Costa’s vitriolic behaviors, it’s readily apparent that Mourinho needs to first put out his own fires before starting new ones halfway across London.

Wenger’s Arsenal team, unlike Mourinho’s “minus one out of ten” Chelsea squad, found a way to clamber back to their imperious form this past weekend as they laid a 5–2 shellacking upon Leicester City at King Power Stadium.

Even Spurs got in on the act — no, really! — by handing a stacked and loaded Manchester City side an epic 4–1 beatdown that will live long in Spurs history. The demolition of City at White Hart Lane also allowed Tottenham to nestle right back into their sweet spot: fifth place.

IMAGE FROM CLOUDFRONT

While Manuel Pellegrini’s men are left to rue the Spurs beatdown, they’ll find some hope in the fact that they’ll be facing a bottom-of-the-table Newcastle at the Etihad next week, which should provide the perfect opportunity for the Citizens to course-correct their current slide in form. But, you never know: they could just pull a Chelsea and draw.

Against Man City, Spurs striker Harry Kane was finally able to bag his first goal of the EPL season — no, really! — after leaving the last six EPL games empty-handed. However, replays clearly showed Kane was offsides when he struck the ball, putting his first goal of the season in some controversy. Someone who also scored a wonky first goal was United’s Captain Wazza, who chested(?) in United’s second goal from an Anthony Martial pass just after half-time.

That goal effectively ended any hopes Sunderland had of leveling the match. For a Sunderland team that plays in the Stadium of “Light,” and whose bright neon-green kits easily overshadowed those of United’s classic red, its play was ultimately too heavy to flourish and too dim to overcome. The Black Cats’ roster has many of the same problems: It’s filled with the dimming stars of players that were highly regarded not so long ago.

Remember the French sensation going into Euro 2012 named Yann M’Vila? Or, the “much improved” and “late bloomer” that was former Spurs player Younès Kaboul? Or, the exiled Liverpool and Italy striker Fabio Borini? Or, the English talents Adam Johnson and Jack Rodwell, who once plied their trade for Man City and Everton, respectively? All five of those promising players (once upon a time) currently find themselves on a Sunderland club that’s toiling away in the dungeons of the relegation zone.

That’s the interesting part about talent — all five of them had ample amounts of talent, but with the help of untimely injuries, behavioral issues, and just being on the wrong team, every single one of them, via different roads, ended up on Sunderland. Funny how the span of just three or four years in world football can make someone either the “next big thing” or, just as easily, the “could’ve-should’ve-been-the-next-big-thing.”

Even those players that have become the “next big thing” recently — like Isco Alarcón at Real Madrid, or Neymar at Barcelona, or Paul Pogba at Juventus, or Kevin De Bruyne at Manchester City — know that their rise is seeped in luck as much as it is in their own talents and hard work. Just like in life, being at the right place at the right time does wonders for any young player and, from the signs of it, those four young stars are playing for the perfect teams.

“Much better instead to see in the Premier League’s moneyed but compelling short-termism as a version of the old Hollywood star system, with the top clubs like studios ready to hoover up, over-promote and every now and then propel to glorious superstardom a great, groaning, glitzy natural wastage of youthful talent. Kevin De Bruyne’s in! Kevin De Bruyne’s out! Wait! Get me Kevin De Bruyne again!”

The Premier League does this very effectively.”

— Barney Ronay, The Guardian, “Manchester United’s Anthony Martial is worth the hype-tinted spectacles”

For every Pogba or every Isco, there are hundreds (if not thousands) of Jack Rodwells and Yann M’Vilas whose careers simply did not pan out, despite their preternatural talent and potential at one point. That thought, more than anything, was what kept playing repeatedly in my mind as I watched Sunderland wilt away at Old Trafford on Saturday.

It’s that ephemerality of football that we both hate and love. And, just last week, we saw Luke Shaw’s young career hit yet another road-bump due to a horrendous injury that will now sideline him for at least six more months. This past weekend, Bournemouth’s Calum Wilson suffered a freak injury as well that will sideline him for a while. Even the living legends are not immune to the frailties of the human body: Lionel Messi went off injured over the weekend, too.

IMAGE FROM OOYALA

So, while I found Louis van Gaal’s “I’m a happy coach” comments a bit comical initially, in this perspective of a fleeting football life, they totally make sense: You have to celebrate the little things going right in life, the small victories here and there. Because happiness, like a football career, is fleeting: Here one moment, gone the next.

So, go on, Louis van Gaal. Be happy while you can.

Because this upcoming Sunday you’ll be facing the reawakened juggernaut that is Arsenal. And that fact alone should bring those rising happiness levels right back down.
