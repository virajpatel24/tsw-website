A Twisty-Turny MLB Season

Evaluating the MLB Divisional Races heading into the postseason.

by Yogi Patel

Share Tweet

Beast of the (AL) East

The Yankees took a huge hit when they learned that Mark Teixeira was shelved for the year after a MRI revealed a stress fracture in his leg. He was having a great season, hitting .255/.364/.518, with 31 HRs and 79 RBIs.

The Yanks have also had a great comeback season from the maligned Alex Rodriguez, who has provided great power and consistency in the DH role. I believe A-Rod deserves the AL Comeback Player of the Year award, but we’ll just have to wait and see how the voters ultimately decide to vote on that.

Brian McCann has also had a great year at the plate, as well as behind the plate with this pitching staff. He set a career high in homers (currently at 25) and still has time to add onto that number. The biggest problem with the team’s pitching staff is the overuse of their bullpen. Dellin Betances, in particular, has already pitched 75.1 innings in 66 appearances. While that may not seem like a lot, Jayson Stark tweeted an insightful tidbit about Betances yesterday that caught my eye:

Had the Yankees made more of an effort to acquire Craig Kimbrel or Aroldis Chapman before the trade deadline, it would have taken a taxing load off of Betances and the Bronx-based club would have been able to end games a lot quicker. (Trust me, the game will end a lot quicker with a 3-headed monster coming out of the bullpen.)

The Yankees have 3 games left against the Toronto Blue Jays, with no room for error since they are also three games back from the Canadian division leaders. Will they overcome the deficit and win the division? Maybe. But I think it would be best for everyone involved with the Yanks to simply start thinking about the impending Wild Card game.

NL Central Crown Up for Grabs?

There’s no question that the NL Central is the best division in baseball. It is rich with great pitching and blossoming young talent. I had a hunch the division race would be a good one, but I never imagined it would be this good. The Cardinals have unexpectedly struggled as of late, yet they still lead the division by four games. The Cards have been a great September team lately, which makes their mid-September struggles pretty unusual.

I believe it has to do more with their sputtering offense than anything else. They have scored the fewest runs (580) in that division, but have simultaneously allowed the fewest (465), for a run difference of +115, which is second-best in the majors. This “score just enough” offense will benefit from Matt Adams returning, but they could use Matt Holiday, too. As usual, they’ve done a great job dealing with their injuries and having players like Stephen Piscotty and Mark Reynolds step up in big roles. They have three games remaining against the Pittsburgh Pirates. Three more showdowns are also left against their arch-rivals, the Chicago Cubs.

The pressure to rattle the wounded Cardinals falls to the swashbuckling Pirates and the cunning Cubs. The Cardinals and Giants have been the only two NL teams to make the World Series in the past five years (2010 Giants, 2011 Cardinals, 2012 Giants, 2013 Cardinals, 2014 Giants). While all will be quiet in the Bay area this postseason (given the Giants’ failure to secure a postseason berth), I don’t anticipate the St. Louis-based Cardinals to choke and let anyone else take their hard-earned divisional crown. I still believe the Cardinals are the team to beat in the postseason and, if they return to the greatness they displayed earlier in the season, then they’ll stop at nothing short of a World Series victory.

Wild Wild West

Have things gotten fun in the AL West or what? No one in their right minds would have imagined that the Texas Rangers would be fighting for the divisional crown this late in the season. The Arlington-based club was projected to be about a year away from contention. But they have benefited from a healthy Prince Fielder and another solid season from the aging Adrian Beltre.

The small additions during the season, such as that of Mike Napoli, have paid off in big ways and, arguably, these moves have been much more impactful to the team than the big-money acquisition of Cole Hamels in late July. Don’t get me wrong; I believe the Cole Hamels trade ignited this team into thinking it could contend this year, but the smaller role players have played a much larger role into getting this team into contention.

Since coming back to the sweltering heat of Texas from the unforgiving cold of Boston, Napoli has himself gotten scorching hot and displayed a batting line of .294/.390/.549 with 4 HRs and 7 RBIs. More importantly, he’s been the spark plug and a much-needed morale booster for the clubhouse. Delino DeShields, a Rule 5 draft pick, is also contributing to the Rangers’ cause by hitting .255/.341/.369, with 23 stolen bases to boot.

The Houston Astros, on the other hand, have had an unbelievable season thus far. They have been struggling as of late, but they’re still only 1.5 games behind the Rangers and have 4 games, including tonight, against them. They’ve had great seasons from the bearded maestro Dallas Keuchel, who is the AL Cy Young winner in my opinion, and star rookie Carlos Correa, who is my pick for AL Rookie of the Year. George Springer is back and has been a welcome addition since the Astros missed his athleticism and heart in the outfield. Collin McHugh has struggled as of late, but he’ll get it turned around soon enough. “El Oso Blanco” is up to his same ole tricks, hitting triples and homers all over the place.

Every year there’s always one really close race to watch. This year, all the action has congregated in the state of Texas. The big question: Who will win that division?

My choice would be the Astros, who have the potential to be a surprisingly good team in the postseason.

I’m excited for some postseason baseball! Are you?
