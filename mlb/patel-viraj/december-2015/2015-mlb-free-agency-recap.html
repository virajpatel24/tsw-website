2015 MLB Free Agency Recap

Zach Greinke heads to Arizona, Jason Heyward becomes a Cub, and David Price takes Boston by a storm

by Viraj Patel

Share Tweet

If the 2015 MLB free agency has proven anything, it’s this: Great starting pitchers are overvalued by clubs across the board. Don’t believe me? Try David Price’s shiny new 7-year, $217 million ($31 million per year) Red Sox contract on for size. If that blockbuster deal doesn’t convince you, then how does Arizona snatching away Zach Greinke from the Dodgers for a cool $206.5 million over the next six years sound? Sounds to me like Zach Greinke’s going to need to invest in a new piggy bank. Or, better yet, an entire island.

Either way, while both of those hefty contracts are absolutely deserved, it does make me question the rationale behind a team like the Diamondbacks breaking the bank on a player of Greinke’s preternatural abilities. As the game of baseball worked to flush out all remnants of the steroids era this past decade, the batting average of the entire league has saw itself plummet to .254. This average lies in stark contrast to the steroid era when the league’s batters were mashing in the .265 to .270 range.

As the offensive production has dropped, pitching has become all the more important. MLB’s collective strikeout rate per game has increased every year since 2006. Conversely, the ERA and walks allowed numbers have decreased in that same time span as the league’s pitchers have access to a substantial, subjective strike zone and an endless procession of steroid-less batters. Make no mistake, power hitters like NL MVP Bryce Harper and former AL MVP Mike Trout are still roaming the territory, but even those guys have a more nuanced and well-developed style of play: They can rack up hits, doubles, and triples off of their speed as well.

In fact, acquiring those multi-skilled athletes has become the general direction of the sport. Just look at this year’s World Series champions, the Kansas City Royals, as an example. The Royals fielded a batting lineup that could rack up hits and find creative ways (walks, HBPs, steals, etc.) to not only get on base, but to also keep advancing to the next bag. The Mets’ young triumvirate of Matt Harvey, Noah Syndergaard, and Jacob deGrom were almost at their wit’s end by the time the Royals grabbed the deciding extra-innings victory in Game 5 to seal the deal. Of course, just as the Golden State Warriors of the NBA have exercised a monopoly on effective small-ball rotations, the Kansas City Royals will continue to maintain the lead in MLB’s version of small-ball baseball, which is why investing boatloads of money on once-in-a-generation pitchers like Greinke and Price doesn’t make too much sense.

Those starters rarely go all nine innings and what ends up happening in these tightly-contested games is that a team’s bullpen will determine the outcome of the games. A starting pitcher, no matter how transcendent he may be, will only influence a game up to the sixth or seventh inning on most outings. After that point, if a team lacks a sturdy bullpen (the Mets, for example), then a small-ball team like Kansas City will find a way to climb back and sneak out 1-run or 2-run wins. This is why Boston’s trade with San Diego for all-star closer Craig Kimbrel made so much more sense when compared to their David Price mega-signing.

While teams will always fetch the star pitchers without much thinking, the question about their usefulness throughout a demanding season with 182 games (plus postseason baseball) must be posed. The Washington Nationals’s star man Jordan Zimmerman left our nation’s capital for our nation’s automobile capital, thanks to a five-year, $110 million deal. Will paying $22 million every season to a 29-year-old Zimmerman really help a Detroit team that needs to fill out its bullpen? Likewise, will Jeff Samardzija’s five-year, $90 million contract with the Giants make much sense when the Bay Area team finds that it needs to rely upon a reliable bullpen in order to win an NLCS series? Having said that, I’m sure some team out there will hand Johnny Cueto a six-year, $150 million mega-deal anyways.

Teams heeding my advice and splurging on offensive stars instead have been the Chicago Cubs and the Los Angeles Angels. The Cubs, led by GM Theo Epstein and manager Joe Maddon, filled out their nine-man starting lineup by bringing on Ben Zobrist from Kansas City (four years, $56 million) and stealing star outfielder Jason Heyward from their arch-nemesis, the St. Louis Cardinals, with an eight-year, $184 million contract. Both Heyward and Zobrist will provide veteran leadership, stellar situational hitting, and smart decision-making to a Cubs offense laden with young rising talents like Kris Bryant, Addison Russell, Jorge Soler, Kyle Schwarber, and Anthony Rizzo.

The Angels have chosen to build their offense around both solid veterans, like Craig Gentry, Geovany Soto, and Yunel Escobar, and a rising young star named Andrelton Simmons. On the offensive side of the free agency market, stars like Alex Gordon, Chris Davis, Dexter Fowler, and Justin Upton still are still doing the rounds. The Mets, who lost their core of Daniel Murphy and Yoenis Céspedes to free agency, will be on the lookout for bullpen pitchers after nabbing Neil Walker and Asdrubal Cabrera on the offensive end.

The biggest surprise of the free agency market, however, has come from financially-set powerhouse sides like the New York Yankees and the Los Angeles Dodgers actually sitting still for once despite the alluring temptations of free agency. Both clubs have opted for a series of small and cost-effective roster-building moves that haven’t come attached to an albatross contract or two. Only time will tell if these two teams will begin circling the free agency market in search of star talents in the days to come. But one thing is for certain: These sharks have been surprisingly, distinctively quiet.

I feel a Yankees or Dodgers mega-mega-mega-blockbuster deal for an aging 30-year-old star (or two) coming. Let the (remaining) hilarity ensue!
