2015 Rugby World Cup - Previewing the Quarterfinals

No Stagehands, We’re All Set!

by Nate Preval

Share Tweet

The stage has been set! The World Cup began with twenty teams, but now, the quarterfinals have been determined and it appears to truly be a clash between the Northern and Southern Hemisphere. Understanding the rocky road taken to get to this point, it is truly magnificent how most of these teams have managed to stay healthy, not being completely gutted by high injury. Because they have been playing such physical and intensive matches over the last few weeks, the final two weeks will be a true test to their mental and physical health.

The first match of the weekend - South Africa and Wales - should truly come down to which pack brings the intensity first in the set piece and which backline proves their dominance early. After seeing the rout that was the Springboks’ match against the US, there is plenty to be desired by the team. However, there were some infringements that happened early in that match that show that they are not invincible. The US, did not win a single match in the tournament, but had some chances early on to score points on what seemed to merely be a lack of discipline by the Bok pack.

Fortunately for the Springboks, their back line is proven and stacked, particularly with a true talent and natural star winger in Bryan Habana (who tied Jonah Lomu’s record for most tries in a World Cup). The world will be watching their match at Twickenham, eager to see who will be moving on to the semifinal.

My prediction? Wales has a major opportunity to make a true (positive) mess of any opportunity that South Africa opens for them. If they manage to take a strong hold early, they will come out on top.

The second match, New Zealand and France, reminds everyone of the crushing loss New Zealand faced in 2007 at Cardiff. This match, also in Cardiff, will probably be the most emotional clash we’ll see during these quarterfinals. France has not played well at all since the Six Nations and their coach are under major scrutiny for the constant changes in the starting XV during their tests in between the two tournaments. The French have much to prove as far as their worth after their pitiful loss to Ireland, who merely seemed to outdo them in every facet of the game, leaving France fans (like myself) wondering: vas-tu les Blue?

Unfortunately, being a realist means accepting one’s inevitable fate. Despite the All Blacks having issues in timing and execution all over the pitch and their scrummage being reduced to the push of a donkey (a big, stout, donkey...they’re still the best in the world), New Zealand should have no true issues warding off France in this quarterfinal rematch of powerhouses. The All Blacks’ should clear a differential of at least 20 points.

Ireland and Argentina on the following day will be the most entertaining match to watch as both teams are rather evenly matched. The only difference is Ireland has more experience at the big stage. Without Paul O’Connell, the Irish will have to produce more carries over the gain line as the leading man in the tournament will be out for disciplinary reasons. The Pumas have a knack for making small gains over the line and then inevitably causing an overload, which opens up their backs with plenty of room to run in. Ireland has one of the best defenses at the breakdown in the entire tournament. It should be fun to watch. In the end, however, Ireland will come out with a narrow but definite win.

Finally, the last match - Australia and Scotland - could prove to be an incredible one depending on which sides come out to compete. Australia has not performed at their absolute best, as a lot of Americans saw when the US had a standing chance against them right before the tournament began. And Scotland having performed above expectation should prove to be a close match for the Aussies depending on their speed and mental preparation. Australia has shown to lose their head during the World Cup matches, but they eventually recover, minimizing on their mental errors and capitalizing on their opponents’ mistakes.

Scotland has the speed and overall full sidedness about themselves that show a semi-final potential if they come flying into this match on Sunday and prove to the world that their 9th ranking is merely a misprint. My prediction? Scotland will capitalize early on the mistakes of Australia and the uphill battle of trying to gain ground on a speedy backline will prove too tiring and too difficult. Scotland will win by no more than 10 points.

It shall be an interesting 48 hours of international rugby!

Nate Preval
