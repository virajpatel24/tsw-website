2015 Rugby World Cup - Previewing the Final

Australia vs. New Zealand: Same story, similar actors, but different stage

by Nate Preval

Share Tweet

The 2015 edition of the Rugby World Cup final oddly resembles the Rugby Championship final from earlier this year. It is probably because the same two teams are playing. However, this time around, the stakes are much higher. The world cup arrives every four years while the rugby championship takes place every year. Therefore, every year we see Australia and New Zealand play each other at least once. If you are underwhelmed by this development, don’t worry because you definitely are not alone. (Particularly if you recall the fact that Australia got extremely lucky with a botched match-deciding call last weekend, Scotland truly belongs at this stage.)

Well, that’s that. It’s an anti-climactic tie, but there must be some positives, right? What is different this time around? For starters, six players of the All Blacks plan on retiring at the tournament’s end.

This cast of massive talent and incredible legacy will be putting on that black jersey for their country for the final time this Saturday. Which can mean only one thing: They will be bringing everything they have left in their tanks and truly leaving it all on the pitch for the world to see. This weekend’s final will be different because New Zealand is not accustomed to losing when it truly matters.

Australia, however, has shown the gall and desire to overcome their neighbors in the Pacific after defeating them in the Rugby Championship this past year. They have a winger in Ashley-Cooper who will have faced the All Blacks 29 times in his test career and holds the record for most tries against them at nine. The majority of their success can be attributed to their coach, Michael Cheika, who has singlehandedly turned the club around since he was appointed as head coach last October. With his leadership, they have shown more drive and determination, as the team shows cohesiveness in their numbers. The New Zealand offense has improved by all measures and this improvement complements the team’s already sky-high efficiency in the set piece and defense.

The question is: in this battle of the islanders…who will come out victorious? If New Zealand works the ball out wide and get the Dan Carter we’ve been seeing throughout the tournament, a Ma’a Nonu of old (prior to the arm injury), and a consistent run by everyone on the team, they are conceivably unstoppable. But throughout the tournament, they have been night and day, showing slow starts in the first half and sheer tenacity in the second. Can they hold on to a small early lead for the full 80 minutes?

Australia has had disciplinary issues to start both halves but once they’ve settled in, they’ve been having field days with their opponents (besides Scotland, of course). Can they fend off the reigning champions for 80 pressure-filled minutes?

There truly is no telling who the victor will be. But whoever wins will win by a margin no greater than six points. However, the winning margin of this final match will only pale in comparison to the trash talking and the pure, unadulterated jubilation that will coarse through the streets and pubs of the winning country.

Nate Preval
