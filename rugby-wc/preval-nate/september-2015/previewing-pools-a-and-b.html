2015 Rugby World Cup - Previewing Pools A and B

The unpredictable pool and the "pool of death"

by Nate Preval

Share Tweet

It’s about that time!

The All Blacks arrived in London last Thursday as the tension grows for the World Champions. As you can tell, they definitely traveled in style while sporting the New Zealand brand, since this being quite possibly the only chance any of the teams will have to relax before matches start this Friday.

New Zealand is fortunate to be in a pool with only one major competitor: the Pumas from Argentina, who are, arguably, the only team to really contest the New Zealand scrums over the past few years. But before we dive into their pool and their big match at Wembley Stadium on Monday, the 21st, against Argentina, let’s take a look at the other pools and their standouts moving forward to the opening ceremony.

Pool A

This pool, being arguably the worst to be drawn into (Pool of Death), consists of all five teams being in the top ten rankings internationally. Australia, England, Wales, Fiji, and Uruguay have all consistently shown their strengths since qualifying for the World Cup. As the hosts, England may have the most pressure ahead of them, but may be undergoing that stress with ease since they find themselves opening up the tournament against a young, fearless Fijian side who recently defeated Canada in a ruthless 47-18 victory. England has the size in the pack to compete well in the set piece - particularly the scrums - as well as the experience all over the field. This will be particularly interesting to see for their star, Sam Burgess, who has the size and brute force of a loose forward, but the speed and tenacity of a centre. Fortunately, he’ll be spending most of his time playing center, despite having only one international cap to his name.

The Wallabies find themselves needing a lot of work from their backs as they try to make it into the group stage. Their match against the USA Eagles showed that their pack has the proper technique and the force, however they lacked the mental toughness that allowed the Eagles to have a glimmer of hope at half-time. Although the ending result was more of what was expected, they did not show the dominance we’ve all grown to expect from the Aussies as they merely capitalized on a few critical errors in the set piece.

For Wales, recently defeating Ireland and Italy shows they have the mental prowess to really make a name for themselves as many members of the media have written them off completely in the group stage. They’ve shown great pace in their back line and they are not afraid to take the ball into contact, regroup in the breakdown, and continue to attack. At this juncture, it would not be farfetched to say that this group is Australia’s to lose and the Welsh’s opportunity to win having beaten Fiji just a year ago. And for Uruguay, having defeated Argentina in their test just last month, show massive promise heading into their first match of the tournament against Wales.

For most groups, it would be obvious who would definitely be a guarantee to move on in the tournament. But given that Pool A is the aforementioned “Pool of Death,” ruling on the side of ambiguity would be the wise choice.

Pool B

Despite the obvious social issues in South Africa, this group of 31 players are taking London by a storm in an attempt to stay true to the same aspirations Nelson Mandela had for them and their country in 1995 when they won the Webb Ellis Cup as the host nation just a mere twenty years ago. The coach, Heyneke Meyer, has been under major scrutiny for the lack of players of color to make the squad for the World Cup, let alone test matches as they had lost major matches to Argentina, Australia, and New Zealand in recent competition. Aside from the social aspect, there exists well-evidenced concern in the Springboks’ ability to capitalize on their serendipitous placement into this group. Accordingly, they are in a group with unfamiliar teams in Japan and Samoa, two teams would have unrelenting force in the pack as well as in the back line. South Africa has experience on their side but, if Meyer continues playing men out of their familiar positions, will it aid them once having to play the USA Eagles?

Over the last few years, rugby has grown in the hearts and minds of US citizens. Sports and Fitness Industry Association put out a report last December citing a growth in rugby from 2008-2013 by 81 per cent and it has hit varsity levels at roughly twenty colleges. Something the Eagles lack, however, is the homegrown talent that these colleges produce, but that alone does not guarantee better performances on the international stage, as they took on the Maori All Blacks last November and then the Wallabies this past month. Both matches ended in a gutting score, but their match, particularly the first half, showed plenty of promise. Word of advice moving into their opening match against Samoa: Be prepared for a physical match. Forwards need to be active and conditioned, no use in being tired and on the field. Backs need the ball more and need support. It should be a fun match.

As for Scotland, they have not been able to contend exceptionally well against the powerhouse sides like France and Ireland, though, the Scots have performed well otherwise. To place them equal to the Eagles would be almost fair considering their abysmal performance in the Six Nations Cup this past year. Will they bring a rejuvenated side to the tournament or will their past performances haunt them on the world’s greatest stage?

In all, this pool is South Africa’s to lose and a toss up amongst Japan, Samoa, USA, and Scotland. The top two advance to the Elimination stage. Things to look out for within this pool are poor performances in the back line and lack of control in the set piece. These teams are all technically sound (for the most part) and will look to take advantage of any mental lapses. At this point, every little mistake counts. The American in me wants to say the United States will pull it out and begin the biggest Cinderella story to ever occur in international rugby; the realist wants to say that Samoa may take the ball and run through the competition at this stage. Guess we’ll just have to wait and see.

Enjoy the opening weekend - and the other two groups (C and D) will be discussed in my article next week (stay tuned).

Play on!
