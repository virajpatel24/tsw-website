2015 Rugby World Cup - Previewing Pools C and D

All Blacks rule Pool C while Pool D remains wide open

by Nate Preval

Share Tweet

Pool C

This group holds two sides - the All Blacks and Argentina - that could easily meet in the final. They faced off this past weekend in what was the most interesting match ever (the Japan/South Africa match comes in close second), in which the reigning champs found themselves trailing at half time. No one saw it coming and the atmosphere at Twickenham was simultaneously awkward and electric. This being the opening round of games, nerves were definitely a part of every match of the opening weekend.

The reigning champs found themselves victorious once the 80 minutes had expired, but that did not occur until they had turned on the afterburners and the intensity in both the pack and along the back line, allowing them to finally seal the deal against a tenacious Argentinian side.

In the same pool are Namibia, Tonga, and Georgia. Quite honestly, these three nations have yet to truly stand out in a World Cup. Tonga did attain a surprise upset of France in the 2011 World Cup, however, unfortunately due to the presence of the heavyweights New Zealand, Tonga’s chances will have to be taken Friday against Namibia.

Pool D

PHOTOGRAPH BY PULSE-STATIC-FILES

The favorites to come out of this Pool D are both France and Ireland. The Irish have shown plenty of promise already as they, both mentally and physically, had their way with Canada in a 50-7 rout during opening weekend. That dominant win over the Canadians still does not provide assurances of Ireland’s advancement out of the pool given Japan’s shocking victory over South Africa, which, in turn, could give underdogs Romania a slight confidence boost if they manage to make the rugby super powers of Pool D play against a Romanian-favorable strategy.

Apart from the Romanians, both the Canadians and the Italians should consider revamping their approach as the French and the Irish plan to take the top two spots in the group. Italy, having already been outmuscled quite nearly everywhere on the pitch by France, need to both look to maintain possession in the breakdown and work on the set piece as remedies to their fragmented style of play. These positive shifts in tactics may still not bring success, but by God it is better than what they have done in the past.

Unfortunately for Canada, they may be ruled out altogether if they cannot find a way to defeat BOTH Italy and Romania by really, really massive margins.
