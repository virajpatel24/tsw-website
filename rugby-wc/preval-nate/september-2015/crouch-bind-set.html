2015 Rugby World Cup

September 15, 2015

Crouch. Bind. Set!

"Football without Pads... Are you mad??"

by Nate Preval

Share Tweet

For most people of the Earth community, the month of September merely represents the change in seasons. The Western Hemisphere is currently preparing to experience the vibrant change in foliage, as fall is surely upon us. However, for a rugby fan (as well as player and coach), this September represents the start of the Rugby World Cup. Very much like soccer, the world cup for rugby comes every four years. This year’s world cup is anticipated to be quite the spectacle for a variety of reasons such as the change in scrum laws, the change in the amount of contact allowed in both the breakdown and in open play, as well as the new focus on health and assessment of injuries.

The Modern Scrum

PHOTOGRAPH BY WWW.INDEPENDENT.CO.UK

For those that do not know, the scrummage (or scrum for short) is the reorganization of play after a penalty is committed. The most common penalty that results in the formation of a scrum is a knock­on: When a ball carrier drops the ball in a forward direction due to the inability to properly handle or catch the ball. The above photo represents what the old scrum looks like. World Rugby (or the IRB) had changed the law in 2012 due to the injuries and constant need to reset and re­engage. Officials now require the forwards from both teams (numbers 1 through 8 usually), to set themselves closer to their opponents, alleviating the potential for the scrum to result in the collapse that you see in the same photo provided above.

As you can tell, the typical scrum looks extremely uncomfortable. In the past, the scrum cadence pronounced by the referee was crouch (a call to get low), touch (a call to ensure decent proximity to the opposition, pause (adjustment to explosive position), and engage. This caused many errors in the positioning of the front row players, inevitably causing a collapse as shown in the photo above between Australia and Wales. Australia has utilized the new scrum laws as their own, having one of the best scrums in the world. One thing that is not realized about the game of rugby is the fact that being stronger, faster, more agile, etc. will yield very decent results. However, the overarching factor that is needed to succeed is leverage. The same is true for the scrums. The lower the scrummage, the more likely you are to win the ball to your side and be off again in attack.

Now, the new cadence is precisely the title of this section: Crouch, Bind, and Set. The referees are showing more responsibility for the stability and safety in the scrums, ensuring that the front row players are close enough to the opposing scrum. This removes the ability to explode into the other players and, in contrast, focuses more on the position of the scrum and the leverage, if any. Instead of constant resets and more time wasted in the game focused on the set piece, the resets are due to slippage and usually improper positioning.

Controlled Contact

In all sports, there has been a growth of concern for safety over the past few years. As one could already tell, it is impossible to avoid contact in rugby. Fortunately, unlike their American football counterpart, the game of rugby has many laws to protect the players when taking contact. There are two in particular that are of utmost importance. Firstly, a tackle is only considered made when the player on defense wraps up the ball carrier or shows legitimate attempt to do so. Failure to do so results in a penalized offense, often times giving the attacking team advantage to play on. Secondly, any attempts at a tackle must be made below the sternum. High tackles are a definite penalized offense and the more cynical or blatant the attempt, the more likely it results in a yellow card and ten minutes in the sin bin. The sin bin – not unlike the penalty box found in NHL games across the United States and Canada – is where players are detained when given a yellow card. Like soccer, a red card results in an early shower and the offender’s team is reduced to fourteen men.

After the tackle, the attacking team must position themselves to retain position by forming what is called a ruck. Again, leverage is key but as the game develops, you see less and less technical work and more of the players expeditiously making their way to the tackled player and stepping over their body, forming a barrier. The tackled player’s body positioning forms the gain line, or where needs to be passed to show forward progression, very similar to what a line of scrimmage provides in American football. The defense can steal the ball from the ruck if the attacking team is not quick enough to form the ruck or strong enough to hold the other team away.

As seen above, there are six players involved in the breakdown currently: the tackled Australian player, the Wales tackler, two Australian players positioning their centers of gravity lower than the two other Wales defenders attempting to get to the ball. The ball carrier is required to release the ball after the tackle is made and proper rugby technique is to release the ball to your team’s side, hence making the ruck possible. Releasing to the other team is NOT how one can keep the attack going in one direction.

Moving along, the attack would continue once a player from Australia places two hands on the ball, signifying the attack is continuing. The defense is then allowed to come across the gain line mentioned earlier and make a play for the ball, as open play suggests.

There is plenty more to the game of rugby, but as far as this article is concerned, this is simply a warm up as the World Cup starts this Friday as England, the host country, takes on Fiji. The rugby community has been awaiting this moment for a seemingly long four years. The only thing that still begs question is: Will the All Blacks retain their title as World champions after losing matches in the past four years to only two teams in South Africa and Australia? Additionally: Will their group stage matchup against Argentina set the tone for the entire tournament?

Stay tuned as each of the groups will be analyzed and survivors will be predicted.

But, for now, play on!
